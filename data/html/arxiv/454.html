Velocity distribution of a homogeneously driven two-dimensional granular gas

Christian Scholz Institute for Multiscale Simulation, Friedrich-Alexander-Universität Erlangen-Nürnberg, Germany    Thorsten Pöschel Institute for Multiscale Simulation, Friedrich-Alexander-Universität Erlangen-Nürnberg, Germany
Abstract

The theory of homogeneously driven granular gases of hard particles predicts that the stationary state is characterized by a velocity distribution function with overpopulated high-energy tails as compared to the exponential decay valid for molecular gases. While this fundamental theoretical result was confirmed by numerous numerical simulations, an experimental confirmation is still missing. Using self-rotating active granular particles, we find a power-law decay of the velocity distribution whose exponent agrees well with the theoretic prediction.

Introduction. Granular gases establish the dilute limit of granular matter. While in many granular systems the particles interact via long-lasting or permanent contact, in granular gases the particles follow ballistic trajectories, interrupted by pairwise dissipative collisions. In a model description, the particles are ideally hard, such that collisions are instantaneous events. Apart from being rather successful to explain the physics of rapid granular flows Goldhirsch (2003) (and many references therein), the concept of granular gases is also of practical importance for the description of natural phenomena, such as the rings of the large planets of our Solar system, e.g. Spahn and Schmidt (2006). Granular gases have been intensively studied for their rich phenomenology, most prominent the cluster and vortex instabilities Goldhirsch and Zanetti (1993); van Noije et al. (1997), which do not have corresponding equivalents in the physics of molecular gases.

To date there is no canonic theory for granular gases, however, much progress was achieved for the homogeneous force-free case, that is, infinitely extended systems in the absence of external forces and boundaries due to confinement. Here the mathematical tools known from the kinetic theory of molecular gases, in particular the (pseudo-) Liouville-operator and the Boltzmann-Enskog equation may be adopted Brey et al. (1997); Goldhirsch (2003); van Noije et al. (1998); Brilliantov and Pöschel (2004) to granular gases which are always in non-equilibrium. Based on this approach, the full hydrodynamics of granular gases can be derived, Sela and Goldhirsch (1998); Brey et al. (1998); Goldhirsch (2003); Brilliantov and Pöschel (2003). Although the homogeneous force-free granular gases are phenomenologically similar to molecular gases, there are numerous phenomena arising from the dissipative nature of particle interaction such as anomalous diffusion Brilliantov and Pöschel (2000, 2005), correlations between the translational and rotational degrees of freedom Brilliantov et al. (2007); Kranz et al. (2009) and others.

The most fundamental characteristics of a granular gas is the velocity distribution function. For v∼vTsimilar-to𝑣subscript𝑣𝑇v\sim v_{T}, where vT=⟨v2⟩/2subscript𝑣𝑇delimited-⟨⟩superscript𝑣22v_{T}=\sqrt{\left<v^{2}\right>/2} is the thermal velocity, it is slightly distorted with respect to the Maxwell distribution which may be described by a low-order Sonine expansion Goldshtein and Shapiro (1995). For large velocities, v≫vTmuch-greater-than𝑣subscript𝑣𝑇v\gg v_{T}, however, the distribution differs qualitatively from the Maxwellian, f​(v)∼exp⁡(−v2)similar-to𝑓𝑣superscript𝑣2f(v)\sim\exp(-v^{2}), by revealing an overpopulated high-energy tail, f​(v)∼exp⁡(−v)similar-to𝑓𝑣𝑣f(v)\sim\exp(-v) Esipov and Pöschel (1997). This tail leaves a pronounced fingerprint in the hydrodynamic fields Pöschel et al. (2006, 2007). By now, the overpopulated tail of the distribution function is theoretically well accepted as it was reproduced using different theoretical approaches Goldhirsch et al. (2003); Noskowicz et al. (2007); Villani (2006) and numerical simulations, e.g., Esipov and Pöschel (1997); Brey and Ruiz-Montero (1999); Puglisi et al. (1999); Moon et al. (2004); Brey et al. (1999); van Zon and MacKintosh (2005).

The experimental investigation of the homogeneous force-free state is difficult for several reasons: (a) starting from some initial velocity distribution, e.g. Maxwellian, the gas needs time (measured in collisions per particle) to develop its native distribution Pöschel et al. (2007). At the same time, the thermal velocity reduces exponentially due to dissipative collisions. For realistic material properties, particle sizes, and collision velocities, there is only a small time window where the gas is still homogeneous and at the same time reveals a well developed distribution function Pöschel et al. (2007); (b) unlike numerical simulations where periodic boundary conditions may be modeled, experiments require always some confinement leading to inhomogeneous density. For instance, solid walls imply an enhanced collision rate and, thus, faster cooling and increased density close to the walls, e.g. Meerson et al. (2004). Therefore, experimental systems must be very large to have invariant conditions at least in the center region of the system, far from the confinement; (c) gravitation impedes force-free conditions. While (c) can be overcome by performing experiments in microgravity Grasselli et al. (2015); Tatsumi et al. (2009); Hou et al. (2008), arguments (a) and (b) persist.

A way out is to consider homogeneously driven systems. Here, random forces are applied to the particles, thus enforcing a stationary state. By the same mathematical analysis as for the force-free case, it was shown that the velocity distribution reveals the same properties Van Noije and Ernst (1998), in particular a high-energy tail, f​(v)∼exp⁡(−vβ)similar-to𝑓𝑣superscript𝑣𝛽f(v)\sim\exp(-v^{\beta}) with β=3/2𝛽32\beta=3/2. Therefore, a corresponding experiment showing the high-energy tail for a randomly driven system would support the theory of granular gases considerably.

Experiments in this direction have been done before Schmick and Markus (2008); Grasselli et al. (2015); Tatsumi et al. (2009); Rouyer and Menon (2000); Baxter and Olafsen (2003); Olafsen and Urbach (1999); Losert et al. (1999); Kudrolli and Henry (2000), in particular measurements for sub-monolayers of spheres on vibrating tables. However, the application of random forces individually to the particles and independent of their position could not be achieved. Instead, in such systems, horizontal particle motion results from out-of-plane collisions of the particles and/or microscopic surface roughness, leading to undesired correlations of the particles. We believe that this is not a well defined way of creating a true two-dimensional granular gas with random collisions, thus, the true origin of overpopulated high-energy tails is difficult to pinpoint.

The groundbreaking idea came from Cafiero et al. Cafiero et al. (2002) who supplied energy to the system by randomly exciting the rotational degree of freedom in a two-dimensional molcular dynamics simulation and showed that the result is independent of the particular method by which energy is injected into the system. The energy was then transferred to the linear velocities through thermalization between the rotational and translational degrees of freedom, thus no artificial correlations were introduced.

In this Letter, we describe an experiment where we investigate a driven two dimensional granular gas where the energy is supplied through random excitations of the rotational degree of freedom. Our result shows clear evidence of an overpopulated high-energy tail, thus confirming the results of kinetic theory of granular gases in the homogeneous regime.

Experimental Setup. The particles used in our two-dimensional experiment are disks resting on circularly aligned titled elastic legs (Fig. 1a).

When placed on an vibrating horizontal table, through an interplay of friction, inertial forces, and inelastic interaction between the particle and the vibrating wall Scholz et al. (2016), these particles suffer random excitations which puts them into irregular rotation. This type of particles was introduced by Altshuler at al. Altshuler et al. (2013) and named Vibrot since it transfers vibrational energy into rotation but not into linear motion in the plane of the table, due to the symmetry of the particles. The intensity of the stochastic kicks can be controlled by the parameters of the vibration Altshuler et al. (2013); Scholz and Pöschel (2016). It depends also on the geometry of the particle, in particular the number and inclination of the legs, and on the particle material Scholz et al. (2016). The particle shown in Fig. 1a is decorated by a white cross on the top to conveniently determine its velocity and position and also by small cantilevers to enforce stochastic kicks and thus accelerate the thermalization between rotational and translational motion. Large numbers (N≥500𝑁500N\geq 500) of such particles of high precision can be conveniently manufactured using 3d printers from acrylonitrile butadiene styrene (ABS) (density ρ=1.07​g/mm3𝜌1.07gsuperscriptmm3\rho=1.07\,\text{g}/\text{mm}^{3}, Young modulus Y=2.3​GPa𝑌2.3GPaY=2.3\,\text{GPa}). The typical coefficient of restitution of the disks is ε=0.5𝜀0.5\varepsilon=0.5 Scholz et al. (2016).

In the experiment, the particles move on a smooth circular acrylic baseplate (diameter 480​mm480mm480\,\text{mm}, width 30​mm30mm30\,\text{mm}) attached to an electromagnetic shaker and leveled horizontally to an accuracy of 10−3∘superscriptsuperscript103{10^{-3}}^{\circ}, to avoid gravitational drift. We used a flower shaped lateral confinement as used by Deseigne et al. (2010, 2012) to avoid creeping motion along the container walls(see also Tsai et al. (2005)) as the flower-like border would re-inject creeping particles back into bulk. Figure 1b shows a top view of the system. The traces of some particles are overlaid to show that the particles indeed perform a diffusive random walk driven by random kicks. No directed linear motion can be observed, see also Altshuler et al. (2013).

The motion of the particles is recorded using a high-speed camera (500 frames/s). Using standard image recognition methods we track the positions of the particles with sub-pixel accuracy Crocker and Grier (1996) and subsequently we compute their linear and angular velocities.

Note that the system considered here is a two-dimensional granular gas. The vibration in vertical direction provides only the energy to drive the particles. Their motion in the horizontal plane is not directly affected by the vibration. Thus, the values of the amplitude and frequency are unimportant as long as they are chosen large enough such that the rotation of the particles is driven sufficiently and small enough such that the particles do not noticeably leave the horizontal plane. The choice f=50​Hz𝑓50Hzf=50\,\text{Hz} and A=0.17​mm𝐴0.17mmA=0.17\,\text{mm} fulfills these conditions, that is, the results do not noticeably depend on f𝑓f and A𝐴A Altshuler et al. (2013); Scholz and Pöschel (2016); Scholz et al. (2016). The same applies to the coefficient of restitution since the predicted overpopulated tail of the distribution function is invariant for any value of ε𝜀\varepsilon, e.g. Pöschel et al. (2007).

One-particle properties. Before studying the velocity distribution function, we check carefully whether the preconditions for the comparison with the theoretical results are fulfilled, namely (a) single particles are required to obey a Maxwellian velocity distribution and (b) the system shall be homogeneous and isotropic.

Figure 2a shows the velocity distribution of an isolated particle without interaction with other particles or the system boundary. The distribution of the x−limit-from𝑥x-component of the velocities (where x𝑥x is an arbitrarily chosen horizontal direction) is a Gaussian with very good accuracy (solid line in Fig. 2a). Together with the isotropy of the system, to be shown below, this gives rise to a Maxwell distribution of the absolute values of the velocities.

For completeness, Fig. 2b shows the distribution of the rotational velocity, P​(ω)𝑃𝜔P(\omega), which is shifted due to the asymmetric inclination of the particle’s legs. Its tails follow a Gaussian. Note that P​(ω)𝑃𝜔P(\omega) is irrelevant for the results presented here, due to the arguments given above. Clearly, the distribution functions are well compatible with the theoretical result, P​(v)∼exp⁡(−vβ)similar-to𝑃𝑣superscript𝑣𝛽P\left(v\right)\sim\exp\left(-v^{\beta}\right) with β=3/2𝛽32\beta=3/2 but not with the equilibrium distribution of a molecular gas, β=2𝛽2\beta=2. This is in excellent agreement with the results from the kinetic theory of homogeneously driven granular gases van Noije et al. Conclusion. One of the most fundamental characteristics of a homogeneous granular gas is the overpopulation of the high-energy tail of the velocity distribution function. This characteristic distinguishes granular gases from molecular gases which obey a Maxwellian distribution, P​(v)∼exp⁡(−v2)similar-to𝑃𝑣superscript𝑣2P\left(v\right)\sim\exp\left(-v^{2}\right), for large v𝑣v. The overpopulation of the high-energy tail was predicted for both homogeneously cooling and randomly heated gas, leading to high-energy asymptotics, P​(v)∼exp⁡(−vβ)similar-to𝑃𝑣superscript𝑣𝛽P\left(v\right)\sim\exp\left(-v^{\beta}\right), with β=1𝛽1\beta=1 in the former case and β=3/2𝛽32\beta=3/2 in the latter. Both results use the same mathematical apparatus Esipov and Pöschel (1997); van Noije et al. (1997), thus, they support one another. Despite the fundamental importance of this result evidenced by the large number of papers referring to it, by now, there was no clean experimental confirmation, due to the fact that the available experiments introduce undesired correlations in an uncontrolled way, either by off-plane collisions or surface roughness in vibrated sub-monolayers or by boundary effects in microgravity experiments. In previous experiments, the overpopulation was observed, however, whether it results from the dissipative collisions in a granular gas or from velocity correlations introduced through the experimental setup, was not free of doubt.

In the experiment presented here, we heat the granular gas by means of the rotational degree of freedom. This idea was introduced by Cafiero et al. Cafiero et al. (2002) in the context of numerical simulations. First, we carefully checked that the system fulfills the preconditions under which the theoretical results were obtained, namely (a) a single particle (which cannot dissipatively interact with others) obeys a Gaussian distribution for the linear velocity components, and (b) homogeneity and isotropy of the many-particle system. Once convinced that the preconditions hold true, we measured the velocity distribution function and found an high-energy tail in excellent agreement with the theoretical prediction, P​(v)∼exp⁡(−v3/2)similar-to𝑃𝑣superscript𝑣32P\left(v\right)\sim\exp\left(-v^{3/2}\right).

This experimental result confirms the theoretical prediction and, thus, the kinetic theory of granular gases being the mathematical apparatus involved in this prediction. This conclusion is not trivial, in our opinion, since the kinetic theory of granular gases employs several assumptions such as molcular chaos, the instantaneous collision assumption and others whose validity for granular gases has been questioned.
