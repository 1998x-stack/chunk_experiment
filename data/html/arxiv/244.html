Quantum SDP Solvers: Large Speed-ups, Optimality, and Applications to Quantum Learning

Fernando G. S. L. Brandão    Amir Kalev    Tongyang Li    Cedric Yen-Yu Lin    Krysta M. Svore    Xiaodi Wu
Abstract

We give two new quantum algorithms for solving semidefinite programs (SDPs) providing quantum speed-ups. We consider SDP instances with m𝑚m constraint matrices, each of dimension n𝑛n, rank at most r𝑟r, and sparsity s𝑠s. The first algorithm assumes an input model where one is given access to an oracle to the entries of the matrices at unit cost. We show that it has run time O~​(s2​(m​ϵ−10+n​ϵ−12))~𝑂superscript𝑠2𝑚superscriptitalic-ϵ10𝑛superscriptitalic-ϵ12\tilde{O}(s^{2}(\sqrt{m}\epsilon^{-10}+\sqrt{n}\epsilon^{-12})), with ϵitalic-ϵ\epsilon the error of the solution. This gives an optimal dependence in terms of m,n𝑚𝑛m,n and quadratic improvement over previous quantum algorithms (when m≈n𝑚𝑛m\approx n). The second algorithm assumes a fully quantum input model in which the input matrices are given as quantum states. We show that its run time is O~​(m+poly⁡(r))⋅poly⁡(log⁡m,log⁡n,B,ϵ−1)⋅~𝑂𝑚poly𝑟poly𝑚𝑛𝐵superscriptitalic-ϵ1\tilde{O}(\sqrt{m}+\operatorname{poly}(r))\cdot\operatorname{poly}(\log m,\log n,B,\epsilon^{-1}), with B𝐵B an upper bound on the trace-norm of all input matrices. In particular the complexity depends only polylogarithmically in n𝑛n and polynomially in r𝑟r.

We apply the second SDP solver to learn a good description of a quantum state with respect to a set of measurements: Given m𝑚m measurements and a supply of copies of an unknown state ρ𝜌\rho with rank at most r𝑟r, we show we can find in time m⋅poly⁡(log⁡m,log⁡n,r,ϵ−1)⋅𝑚poly𝑚𝑛𝑟superscriptitalic-ϵ1\sqrt{m}\cdot\operatorname{poly}(\log m,\log n,r,\epsilon^{-1}) a description of the state as a quantum circuit preparing a density matrix which has the same expectation values as ρ𝜌\rho on the m𝑚m measurements, up to error ϵitalic-ϵ\epsilon. The density matrix obtained is an approximation to the maximum entropy state consistent with the measurement data considered in Jaynes’ principle from statistical mechanics.

As in previous work, we obtain our algorithm by "quantizing" classical SDP solvers based on the matrix multiplicative weight update method. One of our main technical contributions is a quantum Gibbs state sampler for low-rank Hamiltonians, given quantum states encoding these Hamiltonians, with a poly-logarithmic dependence on its dimension, which is based on ideas developed in quantum principal component analysis. We also develop a "fast" quantum OR lemma with a quadratic improvement in gate complexity over the construction of Harrow et al. [harrow2017sequential]. We believe both techniques might be of independent interest.

1 Introduction

Motivation. Semidefinite programming has been a central topic in the study of mathematical optimization, theoretical computer science, and operations research in the last decades. It has become an important tool for designing efficient optimization and approximation algorithms. The power of semidefinite programs (SDPs) lies in their generality (that extends the better-known linear programs (LPs)) and the fact that they admit polynomial-time solvers.

It is natural to ask whether quantum computers can have advantage in solving this important optimization problem. In Ref. [brandao2016quantum], Brandão and Svore provided an affirmative answer, giving a quantum algorithm with worst-case running time O~​(m​n​s2​(R​R~/ε)32)~𝑂𝑚𝑛superscript𝑠2superscript𝑅~𝑅𝜀32\tilde{O}(\sqrt{mn}s^{2}(R\tilde{R}/\varepsilon)^{32}) 111O~~𝑂\tilde{O} hides factors that are polynomial in log⁡m𝑚\log m and log⁡n𝑛\log n., where n𝑛n and s𝑠s are the dimension and row sparsity of the input matrices, respectively, m𝑚m the number of constraints, ϵitalic-ϵ\epsilon the accuracy of the solution, and R,R~𝑅~𝑅R,\tilde{R} upper bounds on the norm of the optimal primal and dual solutions. This is a polynomial speed-up in m𝑚m and n𝑛n comparing to the two state-of-the-art classical SDP-solvers [lee2015faster, arora2007combinatorial] (with complexity O~​(m​(m2+nω+m​n​s)​poly⁡log⁡(R/ϵ))~𝑂𝑚superscript𝑚2superscript𝑛𝜔𝑚𝑛𝑠poly𝑅italic-ϵ\tilde{O}(m(m^{2}+n^{\omega}+mns)\operatorname{poly}\log(R/\epsilon)) [lee2015faster], where ω𝜔\omega is the exponent of matrix multiplication, and O~​(m​n​s​(R​R~/ε)4+n​s​(R​R~/ε)7)~𝑂𝑚𝑛𝑠superscript𝑅~𝑅𝜀4𝑛𝑠superscript𝑅~𝑅𝜀7\tilde{O}(mns(R\tilde{R}/\varepsilon)^{4}+ns\left(R\tilde{R}/\varepsilon\right)^{7}) [arora2007combinatorial]), and beating the classical lower bound of Ω​(m+n)Ω𝑚𝑛\Omega(m+n) [brandao2016quantum]. The follow-up work by van Apeldoorn et al. [vanApeldoorn2017quantum] improved the running time giving a quantum SDP solver with complexity O~​(m​n​s2​(R​R~/ϵ)8)~𝑂𝑚𝑛superscript𝑠2superscript𝑅~𝑅italic-ϵ8\tilde{O}(\sqrt{mn}s^{2}(R\tilde{R}/\epsilon)^{8}). In terms of limitations, Ref. [brandao2016quantum] proved a quantum lower bound Ω​(m+n)Ω𝑚𝑛\Omega(\sqrt{m}+\sqrt{n}) when R,R~,s,ϵ𝑅~𝑅𝑠italic-ϵR,\tilde{R},s,\epsilon are constants; stronger lower bounds can be proven if R𝑅R and/or R~~𝑅\tilde{R} scale with m𝑚m and n𝑛n [vanApeldoorn2017quantum]. We note all these results are shown in an input model in which there is an oracle for the entry of each of the input matrices (see Oracle 1.1 below for a formal definition).

In this paper, we investigate quantum algorithms for SDPs (i.e., quantum SDP solvers) further in the following two perspectives: (1) the best dependence of parameters, especially the dimension n𝑛n and the number of constraints m𝑚m; (2) whether there is any reasonable alternative input model for quantum SDP solvers and what is its associated complexity. To that end, let us first formulate the precise SDP instance in our discussion.

1.1 Quantum SDP solvers with optimal dependence on m𝑚m and n𝑛n

Existing quantum SDP solvers [brandao2016quantum, vanApeldoorn2017quantum] have close-to-optimal dependence on some key parameters but poor dependence on others. Seeking optimal parameter dependence has been an important problem in the development of classical SDP solvers and has inspired many new techniques. It is thus well motivated to investigate the optimal parameter dependence in the quantum setting. Remark 1.5.

Even though our result achieves the optimal dependence on m𝑚m and n𝑛n, it is nontrivial to obtain quantum speed-ups by directly applying our quantum SDP solvers to SDP instances from classical combinatorial problems. The major obstacle is the poly-dependence on 1/ϵ1italic-ϵ1/\epsilon, whereas, for interesting SDP instances such as Max-Cut, 1/ϵ1italic-ϵ1/\epsilon is linear in n𝑛n. In fact, the general framework of the classical Arora-Kale SDP solver also suffers from the poly-dependence on 1/ϵ1italic-ϵ1/\epsilon and cannot be applied directly either. Instead, one needs to specialize the design of SDP solvers for each instance to achieve better time complexity.

Extending this idea to quantum seems challenging. One difficulty is that known classical approaches require explicit information of intermediate states, which requires Ω​(n)Ω𝑛\Omega(n) time and space even to store. It is not clear how one can directly adapt classical approaches on intermediate states when stored as amplitudes in quantum states, which is the case for our current SDP solvers. It seems to us that a resolution of the problem might require an independent tool beyond the scope of this paper. We view this as an important direction for future work.

However, our quantum SDP solvers are sufficient for instances with mild 1/ϵ1italic-ϵ1/\epsilon, which are natural in the context of quantum information, such as learnability of the quantum state problem (elaborated in Section LABEL:sec:learn_intro) as well as examples in [vAG18]. For those cases, we do establish a quantum speed-up as any classical algorithm needs at least linear time in n𝑛n and/or m𝑚m.

1.2 Quantum SDP solvers with quantum inputs

Given the optimality of the algorithm presented before (in terms of m𝑚m and n𝑛n), a natural question is to ask about the existence of alternative input models, which can be justified for specific applications, and at the same time allows more efficient quantum SDP solvers. This is certainly a challenging question, but we can get inspiration from the application of SDPs in quantum complexity theory (e.g., Refs. [jain2011qip, gutoski2012parallel]) and quantum information (e.g., Refs. [aaronson2007learnability, aaronson2017quantum]). In these settings, input matrices of SDP instances, with dimension 2ℓsuperscript2ℓ2^{\ell}, are typically quantum states and/or measurements generated by poly⁡(ℓ)polyℓ\operatorname{poly}(\ell)-size circuits on ℓℓ\ell qubits. For the sake of these applications, it might be reasonable to equip quantum SDP solvers with the ability to leverage these circuit information, rather than merely allowing access to the entries of the input matrices.

In this paper, we propose a truly quantum input model in which we can construct quantum SDP solvers with running time only poly-logarithmic in the dimension. We note that such proposal was mentioned in an earlier version of Ref. [brandao2016quantum], whose precise mathematical form and construction of quantum SDP solvers were unfortunately incorrect, and later removed. Note that since we consider a non-standard input model in this section, our results are incomparable to those in the plain input model. We argue for the relevance of our quantum input model, by considering an applications of the framework to the problem of learning quantum states in Section LABEL:sec:learn_intro.
