The ratios between the moments of inertia of triaxial nuclei: Comment on the Physical Review C 95, 064315 (2017)

S. Frauendorf Department of Physics, University of Notre Dame, South Bend, Indiana 46556, USA

In Ref. Tanabe17 , K. Tanabe and K. Sugawara-Tanabe claim that the transverse wobbling mode, suggested in Ref. FD14 , does not exist. In Refs. Tanabe10 ; Tanabe17 they suggest an alternative model to explain the observed rotational bands. This comment exposes my concerns about their work.

The appearance of wobbling excitations has been suggested as a hallmark for quantal rotation of triaxial nuclei Bo75 . Experimental evidence for wobbling in the presence of an odd i13/2 proton has been found in 163Lu Od01 and in the presence of an odd h11/2 proton in 135Pr Matta15 . The observations have been interpreted by coupling the odd proton with a triaxial rotor that describes the even-even core Ha02 ; Tanabe10 ; Tanabe17 ; FD14 ; Matta15 . The description sensitively depends on the ratios between the three moments of inertia of the triaxial rotor. These ratios are restricted by the indistinguishability of the protons an neutrons which constitute the rotor core. Quantal rotation about a symmetry axis is not possible Bo75 , i. e. the moment of inertia of a symmetry axis is zero. More generally, the larger the deviation of the density distribution from symmetry with respect to one of its principal axes the larger the moment of inertia. This implies that the medium axis has the largest moment of inertia. This fundamental property of the quantal many body system is in stark contrast with the classical rigid body values of the moments of inertia of the triaxial density distribution

Transversality or longitudinalitly are reflected by a respective decrease or increase of the excitation energy of the wobbling band with the total angular momentum of the system. For their version of the particle-triaxial-rotor model Tanabe10 ; Tanabe17 , Tanabe and Sugawara-Tanabe assume the rigid body ratios (1), which assign the largest moment of inertia to the short axis. This scenario (longitudinal according to FD14 ) results in an increase of the wobbling frequency with angular momentum. An angular momentum dependent scaling factor is multiplied to all three moments of inertia, such that the experimentally observed decrease of the wobbling frequency is achieved. Adjusting the triaxiality parameter Œ≥ùõæ\gamma the authors are able to fairly well describe the experimental information on transition probabilities. However, the striking contradiction with the preceding discussion of the ratios between the three moments of inertia raises serious concerns about the suggested scenario. As seen in Fig. 1 (c), the three rigid body moments of inertia are almost the same for the core of 135Pr. The Œ≥ùõæ\gamma dependence of the rigid-body moments of inertia is obviously wrong for weakly triaxial and axial nuclei.
