α𝛼\alpha-cluster states in 46,54Cr from double-folding potentials

Peter Mohr Email: WidmaierMohr@t-online.de; mohr@atomki.mta.hu1122
(Received: date / Revised version: date)
Abstract

α𝛼\alpha–cluster states in 46Cr and 54Cr are investigated in the double-folding model. This study complements a recent similar work of Souza and Miyake Sou17 which was based on a specially shaped potential. Excitation energies, reduced widths, intercluster separations, and intra-band transition strengths are calculated and compared to experimental values for the ground state bands in 46Cr and 54Cr. The α𝛼\alpha-cluster potential is also applied to elastic scattering at low and intermediate energies. Here, as a byproduct, a larger radial extent of the neutron density in 50Ti is found.

1 Introduction

α𝛼\alpha-clustering is a very well-known phenomenon in nuclear physics which is found in many nuclei across the chart of nuclides Hor12 . A lot of work has been done for doubly-magic cores, i.e. 212Po = 208Pb ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha, 44Ti = 40Ca ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha, 20Ne = 16O ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha, and 8Be = 4He ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha. The wide gap between 212Po and 44Ti was often filled by studies of nuclei with semi-magic (N=50𝑁50N=50) cores like 96Ru = 92Mo ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha or 94Mo = 90Zr ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha. A detailed introduction into the nuclear cluster model is provided in a dedicated special issue of Prog. Theor. Phys. Suppl. 132, Ohk98 ; Mic98 ; Yam98 ; Sak98 ; Ueg98 ; Has98 ; Koh98 ; Toh98 . Very recently, Souza and Miyake Sou17 (hereafter: S&M) have extended these studies towards the chromium isotopes. From the QA/ATsubscript𝑄𝐴subscript𝐴𝑇Q_{A}/A_{T} systematics they have identified 46,54Cr = 42,50Ti ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha as preferred nuclei for α𝛼\alpha-clustering. Their subsequent study finds that 46Cr has a significant degree of α𝛼\alpha-clustering, whereas the reduced α𝛼\alpha widths in 54Cr are about a factor of three lower. Interestingly, earlier studies above 44Ti have focused on 48Cr which is considered as a 40Ca core plus two α𝛼\alpha particles Des02 ; Sak02 .

The present study is motivated as an extension of the work of S&M. In the approach by S&M it is first intended to find a two-body potential which is able to reproduce the energies of α𝛼\alpha-cluster states in 46Cr and 54Cr. This potential is then used to calculate the (quasi-)bound state wave functions u​(r)𝑢𝑟u(r) and to derive reduced widths γα2superscriptsubscript𝛾𝛼2\gamma_{\alpha}^{2} and θα2superscriptsubscript𝜃𝛼2\theta_{\alpha}^{2} and transition strengths B​(E​2)𝐵𝐸2B(E2).

The approach of the present study, i.e., the α𝛼\alpha-cluster model in combination with double-folding potentials, has been widely used for the above mentioned doubly-magic cores. Properties of 8Be = 4He ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha and the 4He(α𝛼\alpha,γ𝛾\gamma)8Be capture cross section were calculated in Mohr93 . A detailed study of 19F = 15N ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha and 20Ne = 16O ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha is given in Abe93 which was later extended to (α𝛼\alpha,γ𝛾\gamma) capture reactions Wil02 ; Mohr05 ; Mohr06 . 44Ti = 40Ca ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha and 40Ca(α𝛼\alpha,α𝛼\alpha)40Ca elastic scattering was studied in Atz96 , and the mass region around A≈40𝐴40A\approx 40 was also reviewed in detail in Sak98 . 212Po = 208Pb ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha, the α𝛼\alpha- and γ𝛾\gamma-decay properties of 212Po, and α𝛼\alpha-elastic scattering are investigated in Hoy94 ; Ohk95 , and a study for 104Te = 100Sn ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha is given in Mohr07 . A series of N=50𝑁50N=50 ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha nuclei were investigated in Ohk95 ; Ohk09 ; Mohr08 . Finally, α𝛼\alpha-decay properties of nuclei, including superheavy nuclei up to A≈300𝐴300A\approx 300, are often described within the folding potential approach (e.g., Mohr00 ; Xu06 ; Mohr17 ).

Here I focus on 46Cr and 54Cr and compare the results of S&M to the results from systematic double-folding potentials. Contrary to the approach of S&M who are able to describe the excitation energies of all states in the ground state band using a specially shaped potential, the double-folding potentials of the present study require a minor L𝐿L-dependent adjustment to each state under study. This disadvantage of the folding potential is compensated by two advantages: (i𝑖i) The folding potentials can describe not only bound state properties, but also elastic scattering over a wide energy range. (i​i𝑖𝑖ii) The typically smooth variation of the parameters of the folding potentials can be used to identify peculiar properties of the nuclei under study. Consequently, the approaches of S&M and this work should be considered as complementary.

2 Folding potential model

In the present study the interaction between the α𝛼\alpha particle and the core is calculated from the folding procedure with the widely used energy- and density-dependent DDM3Y interaction veffsubscript𝑣effv_{\rm{eff}}:

For details of the folding approach, see e.g. Sat79 ; Kob84 ; Mohr13 .

The densities ρPsubscript𝜌𝑃\rho_{P} and ρTsubscript𝜌𝑇\rho_{T} of projectile and target are usually derived from the experimental charge density distributions which are measured by electron scattering and summarized in Vri87 . However, in the present study, the density of 42Ti for the 42Ti-α𝛼\alpha potential is not available because 42Ti is unstable and experimentally not yet accessible for electron scattering (although electron scattering for unstable nuclei may become feasible in the near future at the SCRIT facility Tsu17 ). In addition, the charge density distribution of 50Ti for the 50Ti-α𝛼\alpha potential may deviate from the matter density because the N/Z𝑁𝑍N/Z ratio deviates significantly from unity. Therefore, the present work uses theoretical densities as provided as a part of the widely used statistical model code TALYS TALYS .

The following tests have been made to verify the theoretical density distributions of TALYS. First, the calculations of α𝛼\alpha-cluster states in 44Ti of our previous work Atz96 were repeated using the theoretical density of 40Ca from TALYS instead of an experimental density, and only tiny deviations to the results in Tables II and III of Atz96 were found. Second, folding potentials were calculated for 50Ti-α𝛼\alpha using either the theoretical density from TALYS or a matter density derived from the experimental charge density Vri87 . Both potentials were applied to 50Ti(α𝛼\alpha,α𝛼\alpha)50Ti elastic scattering at 25 MeV Gub81 , and it was found that a better description of the experimental data was obtained using the theoretical 50Ti density distribution. Further details on elastic scattering will be discussed later in Sect. 4, including scattering data at low and intermediate energies Rob78 ; Pesl83 . Consequently, after these successful tests, the theoretical density distributions from TALYS were used in the present study.

3 Results and Discussion

The results for 46Cr and 54Cr are summarized in Tables 1 and 2. For comparison, also the results of S&M are listed in Tables 1 and 2. The potentials are shown in Fig. 1.

In general, good agreement is found between the present calculations in the folding model and the previous results by S&M using a specially shaped potential which was optimized for the reproduction of excitation energies. This specially shaped potential of S&M is composed of a Woods-Saxon (WS) plus a cubed WS3 potential (as originally suggested in Buck95 ) which is further modified by a (1+Gaussian) term at small radii. Interestingly, it turns out that this specially shaped potential becomes very similar to the folding potential of the present study except at very small radii (see Fig. 1). Thus, the good agreement between the calculations is not surprising. For completeness it may be noted that specially shaped Gaussian-modified Woods-Saxon potentials have also been applied successfully to elastic scattering e.g. in Mic83 ; Mic00 .

The main difference between the folding potential of the present study and the potential by S&M is the additional Gaussian dip at very small radii in the S&M potential. This dip mainly reduces the energy of the 0+superscript00^{+} ground state whereas states with L>0𝐿0L>0 are practically not affected because of the centrifugal barrier which scales with L​(L+1)/r2𝐿𝐿1superscript𝑟2L(L+1)/r^{2} and thus dominates at small radii r𝑟r (see also Fig. 3 of S&M).

S&M attempt to reproduce the excitation energies of all states within the yrast band by the choice of a specially shaped potential. Contrary to that approach, the folding potential in the present study requires slight adjustments of the strength parameter λ𝜆\lambda to reproduce the energies of the (quasi-)bound states under study. Strength parameters λ𝜆\lambda of about 1.22 to 1.27 are found for 46Cr, and λ≈1.10−1.12𝜆1.101.12\lambda\approx 1.10-1.12 is obtained for 54Cr. The variations of λ𝜆\lambda remain very small (below 4% for 46Cr and below 2% for 54Cr). A smooth dependence of the strength parameter λ𝜆\lambda on the angular momentum L𝐿L of the bound state is found (see Fig. 2). However, there is a significant difference between 46Cr and 54Cr. For 54Cr there is a smooth decrease of λ𝜆\lambda, and all λ𝜆\lambda values for L≥6𝐿6L\geq 6 are practically constant within less than 1%. For 46Cr a clear increase of λ𝜆\lambda is found for L>6𝐿6L>6. A similarly increasing λ𝜆\lambda for large L𝐿L was also found for α𝛼\alpha-cluster states in 44Ti Atz96 .

The L𝐿L dependence of λ𝜆\lambda can be fitted by a parabola

λ​(L)=λ0+Δ​λ×(L−L0)2𝜆𝐿subscript𝜆0Δ𝜆superscript𝐿subscript𝐿02\lambda(L)=\lambda_{0}+\Delta\lambda\times(L-L_{0})^{2} (4)

with the values λ0=1.22027subscript𝜆01.22027\lambda_{0}=1.22027 (1.09596), Δ​λ=7.4881×10−4Δ𝜆7.4881superscript104\Delta\lambda=7.4881\times 10^{-4} (2.6875×10−42.6875superscript1042.6875\times 10^{-4}), and L0=4.1566subscript𝐿04.1566L_{0}=4.1566 (9.7575) for 46Cr (54Cr). The deviation between the parabolic fit in Eq. (4) and the λ𝜆\lambda values in Tables 1 and 2 is typically of the order of 0.001 or below, corresponding to energy shifts of less than 100 keV. Thus, the experimental excitation energies of the yrast bands in 46Cr and 54Cr can be nicely reproduced in the folding potential model in combination with a three-parameter parabolic fit for the potential strength parameter λ𝜆\lambda.

The experimental and calculated level schemes of 46Cr and 54Cr are shown in Figs. 3 and 4. The folding potential with constant strength (λ𝜆\lambda adjusted to the 0+superscript00^{+} ground states) shows a compressed rotational spectrum, in particular for states with small L𝐿L. A perfect reproduction of the excitation energies is obtained using the slightly L𝐿L-dependent strength parameter λ​(L)𝜆𝐿\lambda(L) in Eq. (4). The potential by S&M describes the excitation energies, too.

For completeness it has to be noted that the reproduction of excitation energies (as shown in Figs. 3 and 4) is not trivial. Usual Woods-Saxon potentials show the trend of spectral inversion, i.e. excited states with J>0𝐽0J>0 are located below the 0+superscript00^{+} ground state (e.g., Mohr08 ). Using typical parameters like R=1.3𝑅1.3R=1.3 fm and a=0.65𝑎0.65a=0.65 fm for 46Cr (54Cr), the first 2+superscript22^{+} state is found at −0.160.16-0.16 MeV (−0.220.22-0.22 MeV), and the 12+superscript1212^{+} state appears at −8.628.62-8.62 MeV (−9.889.88-9.88 MeV). The situation becomes even worse as soon as Woods-Saxon parameters are chosen which were adjusted to elastic scattering. E.g., the geometry of the widely used potential by McFadden and Satchler McF66 (R=1.4𝑅1.4R=1.4 fm, a=0.52𝑎0.52a=0.52 fm) leads to excitation energies of the 2+superscript22^{+} state of −0.360.36-0.36 MeV (−0.400.40-0.40 MeV) for 46Cr (54Cr). Thus, results from usual Woods-Saxon potentials are not included in Figs. 3 and 4.

As soon as the potential strength is determined by adjustment of the strength parameter λ𝜆\lambda, the corresponding wave functions u​(r)𝑢𝑟u(r) can be calculated by numerical solution of the Schrödinger equation. Reduced widths γα2superscriptsubscript𝛾𝛼2\gamma_{\alpha}^{2} and θα2superscriptsubscript𝜃𝛼2\theta_{\alpha}^{2}, r​m​s𝑟𝑚𝑠rms intercluster separations ⟨R2⟩1/2superscriptdelimited-⟨⟩superscript𝑅212\langle R^{2}\rangle^{1/2}, and B​(E​2)𝐵𝐸2B(E2) transition strengths result directly from these wave functions (similar to S&M). Qualitatively, the general findings of S&M are reproduced in the present study:
(i𝑖i) The r​m​s𝑟𝑚𝑠rms intercluster separation decreases slightly with increasing angular momentum L𝐿L.
(i​i𝑖𝑖ii) The reduced widths γα2superscriptsubscript𝛾𝛼2\gamma_{\alpha}^{2} and θα2superscriptsubscript𝜃𝛼2\theta_{\alpha}^{2} decrease significantly with increasing L𝐿L.
(i​i​i𝑖𝑖𝑖iii) α𝛼\alpha-clustering is more pronounced in 46Cr compared to 54Cr.
Similar results have been obtained in earlier studies for the neighboring nucleus 44Ti and also for heavier nuclei (e.g., Sou15 ; Ohk95 ; Mic88 ; Buck75 ).

However, there are also some differences between the work of S&M and the present study. The r​m​s𝑟𝑚𝑠rms intercluster separations are slightly larger in the present double-folding model. This holds in particular for states with small angular momentum L𝐿L where smaller values in S&M result from the Gaussian dip at small radii. In the present work the reduced widths θα2superscriptsubscript𝜃𝛼2\theta_{\alpha}^{2} are about 20% higher for states with low L𝐿L in 46Cr and almost a factor of two higher for 54Cr. B​(E​2)𝐵𝐸2B(E2) transition strengths are also slightly higher (of the order of 10%) in the present study. Furthermore, it should be pointed out that the calculated B​(E​2)𝐵𝐸2B(E2) transition strengths agree reasonably well with the experimental data (whenever available). Similar to the work of S&M, no effective charges are required here.

The r​m​s𝑟𝑚𝑠rms intercluster separations require a special discussion. Typically, strong α𝛼\alpha-cluster states are characterized by large r​m​s𝑟𝑚𝑠rms intercluster separations ⟨R2⟩1/2superscriptdelimited-⟨⟩superscript𝑅212\langle R^{2}\rangle^{1/2} (as also found by S&M). 4 Analysis of elastic scattering

Finally, it is interesting to test the α𝛼\alpha-cluster potentials for 50Ti(α𝛼\alpha,α𝛼\alpha)50Ti elastic scattering. Here I first focus on the angular distribution at Eα=25.0subscript𝐸𝛼25.0E_{\alpha}=25.0 MeV by Gubler et al. Gub81 which is available from the EXFOR database EXFOR . This angular distribution covers the full angular range from about 22∘superscript2222^{\circ} to 174∘superscript174174^{\circ} in the center-of-mass system and is thus well-suited for the determination of the optical potential. In addition, it has been shown for the neighboring 40Ca(α𝛼\alpha,α𝛼\alpha)40Ca scattering that the optical potential at low energies (Eα=29subscript𝐸𝛼29E_{\alpha}=29 MeV) is very close to the α𝛼\alpha-cluster potential which describes the 44Ti ground state band Atz96 . For completeness, angular distributions at 104 MeV Pesl83 and 140 MeV Rob78 will also be analyzed. Unfortunately, elastic (α𝛼\alpha,α𝛼\alpha) scattering data are not available for the radioactive 42Ti nucleus.

As a first step of the optical model analysis of the 50Ti(α𝛼\alpha,α𝛼\alpha)50Ti elastic scattering angular distribution at 25 MeV, a phase shift fit (according to the technique of Chi96 ) was performed, and it is found that the experimental angular distribution can be very well reproduced (see Fig. 5). Next, optical model fits have been performed with the α𝛼\alpha-cluster potential as a starting point for the real part. The folding potential which is based on the experimental charge density distribution of 50Ti does not exactly match the diffraction pattern in the forward region. The maxima and minima of the theoretical angular distribution are shifted towards larger angles; this corresponds to a potential which is slightly too narrow. After scaling the radial dependence of the real potential by a factor of w=1.02𝑤1.02w=1.02 a much better fit is obtained (for technical details, see Mohr13 ). Such a behavior is not surprising for 50Ti with N/Z≈1.27𝑁𝑍1.27N/Z\approx 1.27 because the neutron distribution should have a larger radial extent than the proton (charge) distribution. Evidence for a larger radial extent of the neutron distribution of 50Ti was also derived from α𝛼\alpha scattering at higher energies Gils84 .

Fortunately it turns out that the folding potential which is calculated from the theoretical density distribution of 50Ti (as provided in TALYS) is able to reproduce the experimental angular distribution without a significant radial scaling (the best fit is obtained using a scaling parameter of w=0.997𝑤0.997w=0.997, i.e., very close to unity). This confirms the reliability of the theoretical density distribution of 50Ti which is consequently used throughout this paper.

For very pronounced α𝛼\alpha-cluster nuclei like 44Ti or 20Ne such deviations are neither expected nor found Atz96 ; Abe93 , and thus this deviation for 54Cr may be interpreted that 54Cr is not a very pure 50Ti ⊗tensor-product\otimes α𝛼\alpha cluster. This interpretation is in agreement with the smaller reduced widths which are found for 54Cr (in comparison to 46Cr and 44Ti).

As the potential by S&M has been optimized for the description of α𝛼\alpha-cluster states with low JR≈300subscript𝐽𝑅300J_{R}\approx 300 MeV fm3, it is not surprising that this potential cannot reproduce the 50Ti(α𝛼\alpha,α𝛼\alpha)50Ti angular distribution with similar quality as the double-folding potentials. Furthermore, rR,r​m​s=4.32subscript𝑟𝑅𝑟𝑚𝑠4.32r_{R,rms}=4.32 fm of the S&M potential is also lower than the best-fit values around 4.39 fm by about 2%.

At higher energies the differences between the folding potential and the potential by S&M become even more obvious. Indeed, the volume integral JRsubscript𝐽𝑅J_{R} and the r​m​s𝑟𝑚𝑠rms radius of the S&M potential are close to the results of Roberson et al. at Eα=140subscript𝐸𝛼140E_{\alpha}=140 MeV Rob78 . However, angular distributions at higher energies are not only sensitive to the potential at the nuclear surface but depend on the shape of the potential in a wider radial range. In the following I analyze the angular distributions at 140 MeV by Roberson et al. Rob78 and at 104 MeV by Pesl et al. Pesl83 . Unfortunately, the 140 MeV data (as provided by EXFOR) had to be re-read from Fig. 1 of Rob78 ; this leads to increased uncertainties and lower reliability of the extracted parameters. As no uncertainties are provided in EXFOR, a fixed uncertainty of 5% was assumed for the fitting procedure. Furthermore, the angular range of the 140 MeV data is limited to about 75∘superscript7575^{\circ}, and the number of data points (51) is not very high. The angular distribution at 104 MeV Pesl83 covers a larger angular range up to 90∘superscript9090^{\circ}, consists of a factor of three more data points, and is available numerically (including uncertainties) from the internal report KFK-3242 Pesl82 .

The 140 MeV data can be nicely reproduced by the folding potential (see Fig. 6). The resulting volume integrals JRsubscript𝐽𝑅J_{R} and JIsubscript𝐽𝐼J_{I} are close to the results obtained for neighboring nuclei Atz96 : JRsubscript𝐽𝑅J_{R} decreases with increasing energy, and JIsubscript𝐽𝐼J_{I} approaches a saturation value of about 100 MeV fm3. An early study with folding potentials has also determined similar volume integrals Kob84b . Thus, the present analysis mainly confirms that the chosen theoretical density of 50Ti in combination with the DDM3Y interaction is appropriate.

Keeping a fixed real part from S&M and adjusting only the imaginary potential leads to a significantly worse reproduction of the experimental angular distribution. The best fit shows a strongly oscillating angular distribution at backward angles, correlated with very different imaginary volume integral JIsubscript𝐽𝐼J_{I}. It is obvious from Fig. 6 that the strongly oscillating backward cross sections are not sufficiently constrained by the few experimental data points above about 50∘superscript5050^{\circ}. A further local minimum in χ2superscript𝜒2\chi^{2} can be found with reasonable volume integrals (V2 in Fig. 6 and Tables 3 and 4); however, this results in a relatively poor reproduction of the experimental data at forward angles.

Also the angular distribution at 104 MeV Pesl83 is very well reproduced by the folding potential (see Fig. 7). Interestingly, the width parameter for the 104 MeV data is very close (within 0.7%) to the 25 MeV fit. For the 140 MeV data the resulting width parameter was about 2% higher. This finding may have been affected from the uncertainties of the re-digitization of the 140 MeV data and the missing experimental uncertainties.

The fit with the fixed S&M real part results in volume integrals which are very close to the folding potential results. It is not surprising that the fixed S&M real part cannot reproduce the experimental angular distribution with the accuracy as the folding potential with two adjustbale parameters (strength λ𝜆\lambda and width w𝑤w). But even if the same scaling parameters (λ𝜆\lambda and w𝑤w) are applied to the S&M potential, it is not possible to reproduce the angular distribution with a similar quality as the folding potential.

Summarizing the above analysis of elastic scattering angular distributions, it can be stated that the folding potential is able to describe the angular distributions over a wide energy range with smoothly varying parameters. The width parameter w𝑤w remains very close to unity using a theoretical density of 50Ti whereas a folding potential derived from the experimental charge distribution requires a radial scaling of about 2%.

The potential of S&M is close to the folding potential whose parameters have been adjusted to elastic scattering angular distributions. This is a quite noticeable finding because the S&M potential was optimized only for the reproduction of excitation energies of 54Cr. Without any further adjustment of the real part, the S&M potential cannot reach the same accuracy in the reproduction of the angular distributions, but it still provides a reasonable description of the experimental data at 25 MeV and 104 MeV.

5 Summary and conclusions

α𝛼\alpha-cluster states in 46Cr and 54Cr are studied on the basis of a double-folding potential. A minor and smooth variation of the potential strength is found for the states under study in 46Cr and 54Cr. Reduced widths, intercluster separations, and transition strengths are close to the results of a recent study by Souza and Miyake Sou17 using a specially shaped potential, and the calculated transition strengths show reasonable agreement with the experimental values without effective charges. The present study confirms the significant α𝛼\alpha-cluster properties of 46Cr and smaller reduced widths for 54Cr. Furthermore, it is found that the specially shaped potential by S&M is close to the double-folding potential of the present study (except at very small radii).

The application of the double-folding potential to elastic 50Ti(α𝛼\alpha,α𝛼\alpha)50Ti scattering provides excellent fits for the angular distributions over a wide energy range from 25 MeV to 140 MeV. The derived parameters show that the underlying 50Ti density should be preferentially taken from theory. The potential which is calculated from the experimental charge density distribution, turns out to be too narrow by about 2%, thus indicating a larger radial extent of the neutron distribution in 50Ti.
