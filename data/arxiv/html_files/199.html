Rainbow matchings in properly-coloured multigraphs

Peter Keevash and Liana Yepremyan Mathematical Institute, University of Oxford. {keevash,yepremyan}@maths.ox.ac.uk
Abstract.

Aharoni and Berger conjectured that in any bipartite multigraph that is properly edge-coloured by n𝑛n colours with at least n+1𝑛1n+1 edges of each colour there must be a matching that uses each colour exactly once. In this paper we consider the same question without the bipartiteness assumption. We show that in any multigraph with edge multiplicities o​(n)𝑜𝑛o(n) that is properly edge-coloured by n𝑛n colours with at least n+o​(n)𝑛𝑜𝑛n+o(n) edges of each colour there must be a matching of size n−O​(1)𝑛𝑂1n-O(1) that uses each colour at most once.

1. Introduction

A Latin square of order n𝑛n is an n×n𝑛𝑛n\times n array of n𝑛n symbols in which each symbol occurs exactly once in each row and in each column. A transversal of a Latin square is a set of entries that represent each row, column and symbol exactly once. The following fundamental open problem on transversals in Latin squares is known as the Ryser-Brualdi-Stein conjecture111Ryser conjectured that the number of transversals has the same parity as n𝑛n, so any Latin square of odd order has a transversal (see [8]). For any even n𝑛n the addition table of a cyclic group is a Latin square with no transversal. Brualdi made Conjecture 1.1 (see e.g. [2]); Stein [7] independently made a stronger conjecture. (the best known bound is n−O​(log2⁡n)𝑛𝑂superscript2𝑛n-O(\log^{2}n) by Hatami and Shor [4]).

Conjecture 1.1 (Ryser-Brualdi-Stein).

Every Latin square of order n𝑛n has a partial transversal of size n−1𝑛1n-1.

It is not hard to see that Conjecture 1.1 is equivalent to saying that any proper n𝑛n-edge-colouring of the complete bipartite graph Kn,nsubscript𝐾𝑛𝑛K_{n,n} has a rainbow matching (i.e. a matching with no repeated colour) of size n−1𝑛1n-1. Aharoni and Berger [1] conjectured the following generalization.

Conjecture 1.2 (Aharoni and Berger).

Let G𝐺G be a bipartite multigraph that is properly edge-coloured with n𝑛n colours and has at least n+1𝑛1n+1 edges of each colour. Then G𝐺G has a rainbow matching using every colour.

Pokrovskiy [5] showed that this conjecture is asymptotically true, in that the conclusion holds if there are at least n+o​(n)𝑛𝑜𝑛n+o(n) edges of each colour. In this paper we consider the same question without the bipartiteness assumption. We obtain a result somewhat analogous to Pokrovskiy’s, although we require an additional (mild) assumption on edge multiplicities, and we have to allow a constant number of unused colours. (We give explicit constants in the statement for the sake of concreteness, but we did not attempt to optimise these.)

Theorem 1.1.

Let 0<ε<10−30𝜀superscript1030<\varepsilon<10^{-3}, k=220/ε𝑘superscript220𝜀k=2^{20/\varepsilon} and n>k2𝑛superscript𝑘2n>k^{2}. Suppose G𝐺G is any multigraph with edge multiplicities at most n/k𝑛𝑘n/k that is properly edge-coloured with n𝑛n colours so that every colour appears at least (1+ε)​n1𝜀𝑛(1+\varepsilon)n times. Then G𝐺G has a rainbow matching of size n−k𝑛𝑘n-k.

Our proof of Theorem 1.1 is algorithmic. Given any rainbow matching M𝑀M of size less than n−k𝑛𝑘n-k, we construct a hierarchy of edges e𝑒e in M𝑀M for which we can find another rainbow matching M′superscript𝑀′M^{\prime} of the same size which does not contain e𝑒e, or indeed any edge with the same colour as e𝑒e. We find M′superscript𝑀′M^{\prime} by a sequence of switches using edges at lower levels in hierarchy. The switching method is robust, in that there are many choices at each step, and so it is possible to avoid any constant size set of vertices or edges that were altered by previous switches. To analyse the algorithm, we suppose for contradiction that it does not find a switch to increase the matching, and then prove several structural properties of G𝐺G that culminate in a counting argument to give the required contradiction.

We describe and analyse our algorithm in Section 3, after briefly summarising our notation in the next section. Then we give the proof of Theorem 1.1 in Section 4. In the concluding remarks we discuss the relationship of our work to the recent preprint of Gao, Ramadurai, Wanless and Wormald [3].

5. Conclusion

A similar result to Theorem 1.1 was very recently obtained independently by Gao, Ramadurai, Wanless and Wormald [3]. Their result is closer than ours to the spirit of Conjecture 1.2, as they obtain a full rainbow matching (whereas we allow a constant number of unused colours). However, our results are incomparable, as they need a stronger bound on edge multiplicities, namely n/log2⁡n𝑛superscript2𝑛\sqrt{n}/\log^{2}n. Our algorithm is deterministic, whereas theirs is randomised, and the analysis of our algorithm is simpler. In any case, the two approaches are very different, so we think that it will be valuable to pursue both sets of ideas, and perhaps also the approach of Pokrovskiy, in making further progress on Conjecture 1.2.
