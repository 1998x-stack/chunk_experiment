DETECTABILITY OF DARK MATTER SUBHALOS BY MEANS OF THE GAMMA-400 TELESCOPE

A. E. Egorov Lebedev Physical Institute, RU-119991 Moscow, Russia    A. M. Galper Lebedev Physical Institute, RU-119991 Moscow, Russia National Research Nuclear University MEPhI, RU-115409 Moscow,Russia    N. P. Topchiev Lebedev Physical Institute, RU-119991 Moscow, Russia    A. A. Leonov Lebedev Physical Institute, RU-119991 Moscow, Russia National Research Nuclear University MEPhI, RU-115409 Moscow,Russia    S. I. Suchkov Lebedev Physical Institute, RU-119991 Moscow, Russia    M. D. Kheymits National Research Nuclear University MEPhI, RU-115409 Moscow,Russia    Yu. T. Yurkin National Research Nuclear University MEPhI, RU-115409 Moscow,Russia
Abstract

We investigated the detectability of Galactic subhalos with masses (106−109)​M⊙superscript106superscript109subscript𝑀direct-product(10^{6}-10^{9})M_{\odot} formed by annihilating WIMP dark matter by the planned GAMMA-400 gamma-ray telescope. The inner structure of dark matter subhalos and their distribution in the Galaxy were taken from corresponding simulations. We showed that the expected gamma-ray flux from subhalos strongly depends on WIMP mass and subhalo concentration, but less strongly depends on the subhalo mass. In an optimistic case we may expect the flux of 10–100 ph/year above 100 MeV from the closest and most massive subhalos, which would be detectable sources for GAMMA-400. However, resolving the inner structure of subhalos might be possible only by the joint analysis of the future GAMMA-400 data and data from other telescopes due to smallness of fluxes. Also we considered the recent subhalo candidates 3FGL J2212.5+0703 and J1924.8–1034 within the framework of our model. We concluded that it is very unlikely that these sources belong to the subhalo population.

1 Introduction and motivation

Dark matter (DM) in the Universe was discovered over 80 years ago. However, its exact physical nature remains to be one of the biggest puzzles in the modern astrophysics. There is a big variety of candidates on the role of DM. Weakly Interacting Massive Particles (WIMPs) in the form of neutralino probably still remain to be the most popular candidate (see, e.g., Bertone (2010)). As known, these supersymmetric particles can self-annihilate with production of highly energetic Standard Model particles: photons, electrons, protons, etc. Bertone (2010). This process is actively going in any regions with high DM concentration and could manifest itself by an emission from annihilation products. This is very promising direction of DM searches (so-called indirect searches), which are being conducted in any astrophysical objects from the Sun to galaxy clusters. One such an object of interest is DM subhalos in our Galaxy. N-body simulations of DM halos reliably predict an existence of extended hierarchical substructure inside them in the form of subhalos in a very wide mass range down to ∼10−6​M⊙similar-toabsentsuperscript106subscript𝑀direct-product\sim 10^{-6}M_{\odot} Bertone (2010). Thus, the modern simulations (see, e.g., Charles et al. (2016)) of Milky Way (MW) –sized halos are able to resolve tens of thousands of subhalos. The most massive subhalos are expected to host the known dwarf satellites of MW. Smaller subhalos (with masses less than (107−108)​M⊙superscript107superscript108subscript𝑀direct-product(10^{7}-10^{8})M_{\odot} Calore et al. (2017)) may completely lack any stellar/baryonic component and, hence, any astrophysical counterpart on the sky. However, they could be close enough in order to represent bright gamma-ray sources due to DM annihilation. At the same time, ∼1/3similar-toabsent13\sim 1/3 of the sources in the 3FGL Fermi-LAT catalog lack firm associations with known counterparts at other wavelengths Acero et al. (2015). Hence, some of these unidentified sources could be, in fact, DM subhalos. In general, one may expect the following valuable properties of DM subhalos as gamma-ray sources, which allow one to classify them unambiguously Charles et al. (2016):

  • •

    they almost completely lack any emission other than that from DM annihilation;

  • •

    the latter is absolutely stable emission with no variability in time;

  • •

    all subhalos would have the same predictable spectra;

  • •

    they would have almost isotropic distribution on the sky;

  • •

    they might be resolved as extended sources (!), which is rather impossible for typical astrophysical sources, such as blazars.

Thus, DM subhalos represent very exciting targets for indirect DM searches. Naturally, their detectability by Fermi-LAT has been already widely explored (e.g., Calore et al. (2017)). The conclusion of this exploration was not optimistic: Fermi-LAT is able to detect ∼1similar-toabsent1\sim 1 such object on the whole sky and resolve spatially <1 object. At the same time, some groups made attempts to identify DM subhalos in Fermi-LAT point source catalogs (e.g., Bertoni et al. (2016)). These searches yielded a couple of interesting candidates 3FGL J2212.5+0703 and J1924.8–1034 Bertoni et al. (2016); Wang et al. (2016); Xia et al. (2017). Their exact nature remains uncertain, and we will discuss it in Section 4.

Also, it is very timely and relevant now to evaluate a potential of future gamma-ray missions to detect and resolve subhalos (e.g., Chou et al. (2017)). The main goal of our paper is to explore the detectability of DM subhalos by the new gamma-ray telescope GAMMA-400, which is under designing now and planned for launch around 2025 Galper et al. (2013); Topchiev et al. (2017). It is expected to have unique characteristics, such as energy and angular resolutions. The latter is designed to be ∼0.01​°similar-toabsent0.01°\sim 0.01\degree at energies ∼similar-to\sim 100 GeV, which is by an order of magnitude better than that for Fermi-LAT. Hence, GAMMA-400 may have a great potential to detect DM subhalos and resolve their inner structure, paving the road towards a comprehensive exploration of DM nature. Also we may expect a fruitful outcome from the joint analysis of GAMMA-400 observational data together with data from other telescopes - Fermi-LAT, proposed e-ASTROGAM De Angelis et al. (2017) and others. We perform our analysis assuming that DM is constituted by the mentioned neutralinos.

2 Modeling the subhalo population

Here we used the quite comprehensive model of Galactic subhalos developed in Calore et al. (2017). The authors of this work performed N-body simulations of the MW-like halo of two types: with cold DM only and with CDM+baryons. The authors did not notice significant differences in the results of these two cases. The mass range of subhalos, which are resolvable in this simulation, is ∼(106−1011)​M⊙similar-toabsentsuperscript106superscript1011subscript𝑀direct-product\sim(10^{6}-10^{11})M_{\odot}. In our work we focused only on the range (106−109)​M⊙superscript106superscript109subscript𝑀direct-product(10^{6}-10^{9})M_{\odot}, since according to Calore et al. (2017) all the heavier subhalos host stars and, hence, formally represent dwarf satellites. Indeed, the boundary between subhalos and dwarfs is very nominal - these objects share the same nature. However, we plan to study signals from dwarfs in a separate work. Concerning the masses (106−109)​M⊙superscript106superscript109subscript𝑀direct-product(10^{6}-10^{9})M_{\odot}, Calore et al. Also from Figure 1 we may note that the flux strongly depends on the assumed concentration model: the former varies by about an order of magnitude when the concentration is varied between the minimal and maximal cases discussed in Section 2. The solution of this problem could be in merging the future GAMMA-400 data with data from other telescopes - Fermi-LAT, e-ASTROGAM, etc. - and doing the joint analysis. This might allow one to synthesize the spatial image of a subhalo.

4 Implications for the subhalo candidates 3FGL J2212.5+0703 and J1924.8–1034

As we already mentioned in Section 1, the recent works Bertoni et al. (2016); Wang et al. (2016); Xia et al. (2017) confidently reported about a couple of subhalo candidates in 3FGL catalog - J2212.5+0703 and J1924.8–1034. Both of these objects are likely extended with the angular radius ∼0.1​°similar-toabsent0.1°\sim 0.1\degree. Their spectra can be well fitted by WIMP with mass ∼similar-to\sim(20–40) GeV annihilating to b​b¯𝑏¯𝑏b\bar{b}. The authors of Wang et al. (2016); Xia et al. (2017) assumed the thermal annihilation cross section ⟨σ​v⟩≈3⋅10−26delimited-⟨⟩𝜎𝑣⋅3superscript1026\langle\sigma v\rangle\approx 3\cdot 10^{-26} cm3/s when fitted the signals from candidates. We would like to notice that such a big cross section is unrealistic for the mentioned WIMP masses according to the Fermi-LAT constraints Ackermann et al. (2015) - they require the cross section to be by several times smaller. We re-calculated the required J𝐽J-factors (which reproduce the fluxes) for these objects with the realistic cross section values and obtained J⩾5⋅1020𝐽⋅5superscript1020J\geqslant 5\cdot 10^{20} GeV2 cm-5. All the known dwarfs have J≲1020less-than-or-similar-to𝐽superscript1020J\lesssim 10^{20} GeV2 cm-5 Albert et al. (2017). Therefore, the subhalo candidates require abnormally big J𝐽J. It seems unlikely for such objects to not be bright and prominent dwarfs. One opportunity to stay dark for them is to be light, but extremely close subhalos. In this context, we estimated the required distances to these objects based on the measured flux from them. We assumed two representative mass values for the subhalo candidates – 106​M⊙superscript106subscript𝑀direct-product10^{6}M_{\odot} and 109​M⊙superscript109subscript𝑀direct-product10^{9}M_{\odot}. For these cases we obtained the distances ∼1similar-toabsent1\sim 1 kpc and ∼10similar-toabsent10\sim 10 kpc respectively (approximately same for both objects). This clearly contradicts the relation (4). Also one may have a look at Figure 10, right in Calore et al. (2017), where the authors plotted all the subhalos from 100 Monte Carlo simulations of MW. And one can clearly see that no single subhalo out of 100 realizations felt closer than ≈12absent12\approx 12 kpc. Thus, these objects essentially fail both basic tests - by J𝐽J-factor and by distance to them. Indeed, in principle we might be extremely lucky to deal with super big outliers from average trends, which appeared so close to us by a random chance. However, considering Figure 10 in Calore et al. (2017) and the fact that these are two objects - not just one - such a coincidence does not seem to be possible. Instead, these objects are likely typical astrophysical sources composed by two objects projected onto very close lines of sight. This opportunity is absolutely allowed by Bertoni et al. (2016); Wang et al. (2016); Xia et al. (2017). In fact, the data even slightly prefers the binary object hypothesis, since the object images seem to have a mild decline from the circular shape.

These subhalo candidates would be relatively bright sources for GAMMA-400 with the fluxes ∼1000similar-toabsent1000\sim 1000 ph/year above 100 MeV. Indeed, we may include them in our program of observations to study them more. However, based on the considerations above, we do not suppose that these objects are real DM subhalos.

5 Conclusions and discussion

In this work we evaluated the detectability of the Galactic DM subhalos by the planned GAMMA-400 gamma-ray telescope. We focused on subhalos with the most relevant masses (106−109)​M⊙superscript106superscript109subscript𝑀direct-product(10^{6}-10^{9})M_{\odot}. We modeled the distribution of DM density inside the subhalos and the spatial distribution of subhalos in MW based on the results of respective N-body simulations of the MW-like halo Calore et al. (2017). Then we calculated the gamma-ray fluxes from subhalos due to DM annihilation and studied the flux dependence on various parameters (see Figures 1,2). We may outline the following main conclusions.

  • •

    The total flux (above 100 MeV) from the subhalos rather mildly depends on their mass, but strongly depends on the assumed subhalo concentration and DM particle mass. The most massive subhalos are the brightest despite being the remotest.

  • •

    For the case of the most relevant WIMP mass ∼100similar-toabsent100\sim 100 GeV (and thermal annihilation cross section) we may expect ≳1greater-than-or-equivalent-toabsent1\gtrsim 1 objects on the sky with fluxes ∼10similar-toabsent10\sim 10 ph/year. This suggests that the subhalos are marginally detectable by GAMMA-400 even for the case of a quite heavy WIMP.

  • •

    It is difficult to resolve the inner structure of subhalos due to smallness of fluxes. However, this difficulty might be alleviated in the future by the joint analysis of GAMMA-400 data and data from other telescopes - Fermi-LAT, e-ASTROGAM, etc.

  • •

    The recent subhalo candidates 3FGL J2212.5+0703 and J1924.8–1034 require unrealistically large J𝐽J-factors to reproduce their observed fluxes. We suppose that it is much more probable for these sources to be composed of binary astrophysical sources instead of annihilating DM.

Thus, DM subhalos are interesting and promising targets for DM searches by the GAMMA-400 gamma-ray telescope. We plan to continue the research of these objects. Also we plan to study the opportunities to detect and constrain DM in Milky Way satellites by GAMMA-400.
