Abstract

Recent studies indicate that the ground state of a QCD four bottom quark system may form a resonance in the range ∼18−19similar-toabsent1819\sim 18-19 GeV, near the di-ΥΥ\Upsilon threshold, which may be observable at the LHC in the four lepton final state. These studies also predict a variety of possible resonances associated with the various excited states and allow for a number of possible spin and C​P𝐶𝑃CP quantum numbers. Of course, even if a resonance is observed in four leptons in the predicted mass range, it will be prudent to experimentally confirm its four bottom quark QCD nature or whether it is perhaps something more exotic. We initiate an investigation in this direction by exploring the ability of the normalized fully differential decay width for decays to four leptons to probe the underlying nature of a putative resonance including C​P𝐶𝑃CP and tensor properties. We assume the first observed state is a spin-0 boson decaying to at least one on-shell ΥΥ\Upsilon, but allow for decays to four leptons through Υ​Υ,Υ​γΥΥΥ𝛾\Upsilon\Upsilon,\,\Upsilon\gamma, and Υ​ZΥ𝑍\Upsilon Z vector boson pairs. We consider a range of resonance masses around the di-ΥΥ\Upsilon threshold and find excellent prospects at the LHC for establishing its C​P𝐶𝑃CP and tensor properties, perhaps not long after a discovery depending on (unknown) production cross sections and the exact resonance mass.

I Introduction

Recent studies indicate Berezhnoy et al. (2012); Du et al. (2013); Chen et al. (2015a); Karliner et al. (2017a); Chen et al. (2017a); Bai et al. (2016); Wu et al. (2016); Richard et al. (2017); Wang (2017); Eichten and Liu (2017) that a QCD bound state composed of four bottom quarks, a so called ‘beauty-full’ tetraquark Bai et al. (2016), may be observable as a resonance at the LHC in the mass range ∼18−19similar-toabsent1819\sim 18-19 GeV 111Tantalizingly, a recent (not yet approved) CMS analysis Durgut (2018) hints at a potential four lepton excess just in this mass range.. In particular it has been pointed out that decays to an on-shell ΥΥ\Upsilon and a pair of leptons with ΥΥ\Upsilon also decaying to leptons allows for the possibility of a striking signal at the LHC in the invariant mass distribution of Υ​(1​S)​ℓ+​ℓ−Υ1𝑆superscriptℓsuperscriptℓ\Upsilon(1S)\,\ell^{+}\ell^{-} events (ℓ=e,μℓ𝑒𝜇\ell=e,~{}\mu). These studies predict a plethora of bound states around, but just below, the di-Υ,Υ/ηbΥΥsubscript𝜂𝑏\Upsilon,\Upsilon/\eta_{b} thresholds Chen et al. (2017a), thus implying a bottom tetraquark bound state. This includes bound states of spin 0,1,010,1, or 222 carrying different combinations of C​P𝐶𝑃CP quantum numbers, potentially including admixtures. While progress on computing the ground state energy of a tetraquark bound state (or effective mass) has been made, studies of their decays are still in the preliminary Berezhnoy et al. (2012); Chen et al. (2017a); Karliner et al. (2017a); Eichten and Liu (2017) stages.

These studies of potential tetraquark systems are based on theoretical calculations utilizing various sum rules or ad hoc bound state potentials to compute the 4​b4𝑏4b ground state. Generically these calculations have found the ground state to lie below the di-ΥΥ\Upsilon threshold. However, recent lattice QCD calculations based on first principles Hughes et al. (2018) indicate that no 4​b4𝑏4b bound state is expected below the di-ΥΥ\Upsilon threshold. Instead the ground state is found to be on threshold, implying a di-meson system and not a tetraquark bound state. Regardless, whether a putative 4​b4𝑏4b resonance appears above or below the di-ΥΥ\Upsilon threshold there is the possibility for a striking four lepton signal which could be observable at the LHC.

The fact that a potentially narrow resonance 222We comment that the authors in Anwar et al. (2017) studied 4​b4𝑏4b tetraquark bound states and found it would not have a narrow width., can be observed in the precisely measured four lepton channel offers a unique opportunity to study its detailed properties utilizing the differential spectra of the many observables in the final state. Furthermore, even if a resonance is observed in four leptons in the predicted mass range, it will be prudent to experimentally confirm its four bottom quark QCD nature or whether it is perhaps something more exotic. We initiate an investigation 333Recently the authors in Eichten and Liu (2017) also examined the 4​ℓ4ℓ4\ell channel as well as discussed potential production mechanisms of a putative 4​b4𝑏4b bound state decaying through an intermediate Υ​Υ∗ΥsuperscriptΥ∗\Upsilon\Upsilon^{\ast} pair. of this possibility assuming at least one on-shell ΥΥ\Upsilon, but making minimal assumptions of how the effective couplings to Υ​ℓ+​ℓ−Υsuperscriptℓsuperscriptℓ\Upsilon\,\ell^{+}\ell^{-} are generated since these couplings can depend on both perturbative and non-perturbative effects Wang (2017) as well as in principle beyond the SM (BSM) physics.

To this end we utilize a generic effective theory framework to analyze these (potential) tetraquark decays. In particular, we explore the possibility that the ‘off-shell’ lepton pair can come from either an Υ,ZΥ𝑍\Upsilon,~{}Z, or γ𝛾\gamma vector boson. While in the SM we expect the ΥΥ\Upsilon contribution to dominate Bai et al. (2016); Eichten and Liu (2017), we examine whether the fully differential decay width for decays to four leptons can be used to establish this directly. Should the γ𝛾\gamma or Z𝑍Z be found to dominate or contribute significantly, it would be a tantalizing hint that perhaps this is not a SM QCD state and instead something more exotic. As part of this analysis we explore the possibility of probing the C​P𝐶𝑃CP and tensor properties. We also briefly discuss various potential C​P𝐶𝑃CP violating effects which, should a resonance be discovered, would be interesting to investigate further.

For this simplified study we assume the first observed resonance is a spin-0 scalar allowing for various C​P𝐶𝑃CP and tensor structures in its couplings to Υ​Υ,Υ​Z,Υ​γΥΥΥ𝑍Υ𝛾\Upsilon\Upsilon,\Upsilon Z,\Upsilon\gamma pairs. We utilize the matrix element method (MEM) analysis framework developed in Chen et al. (2013); Chen and Vega-Morales (2014); Chen et al. (2015b, 2014a, 2014b); Falkowski and Vega-Morales (2014); Chen et al. (2015c) for scalar decays to four leptons which includes the tree level q​q¯→4​ℓ​(4​ℓ≡2​e​2​μ, 4​e, 4​μ)→𝑞¯𝑞4ℓ4ℓ2𝑒2𝜇4𝑒4𝜇q\bar{q}\to 4\ell~{}(4\ell\equiv 2e2\mu,\,4e,\,4\mu) background. Motivated by the exciting possibility of a tetraquark bound state, we emphasize in our analysis a spin-0 resonance ∼500similar-toabsent500\sim 500 MeV below the di-ΥΥ\Upsilon threshold, but also examine resonance masses just below, at, and above the di-ΥΥ\Upsilon threshold.

We perform various hypothesis tests to examine the ability to directly establish the nature of the putative resonance including its C​P𝐶𝑃CP and tensor properties. We find excellent prospects once 𝒪​(10−100)𝒪10100\mathcal{O}(10-100) signal events have been observed at the LHC which may in principle be possible not long after discovery depending on (unknown) production cross sections and the exact mass of the putative resonance. We leave a study of spin-1 and spin-2 resonances decaying to 2​ℓ+Υ2ℓΥ2\ell+\Upsilon as well as other multiquark states Karliner et al. (2017b) with 4​ℓ4ℓ4\ell signals to future work.

II Decays to Four Leptons

Here we discuss the effective theory framework we use to model the decays to four leptons of a putative scalar resonance. To gain intuition for the discriminating power of the fully differential decay width, we also examine the phase space contributions of the operators in Eq. (2) and Eq. (II.1) as well as some invariant mass and angular distributions in the 4​ℓ4ℓ4\ell center of mass frame Chen et al. (2013); Chen and Vega-Morales (2014).

(2012) as well as the negligible branching ratio into leptons Patrignani et al. (2016). We therefore neglect these contributions to the dilepton spectrum. We have also neglected contributions from the Υ​(2​S)Υ2𝑆\Upsilon(2S) and Υ​(3​S)Υ3𝑆\Upsilon(3S) excited states. However, including these various contributions may be necessary once many four lepton events have been collected.

II.3 One dimensional projections

Since we do not use rate information in our MEM analysis, the discriminating power of the φ→4​ℓ→𝜑4ℓ\varphi\to 4\ell channel comes from differences in shape of the differential spectra arising from the operators in Eq. (2) and Eq. (II.1). To gain intuition for this we examine various one dimensional projections. As discussed above, in the φ𝜑\varphi center of mass frame (or four lepton) there are two invariant masses formed out of the four vectors of the di-lepton pairs as well as five angular variables Chen and Vega-Morales (2014).

The three most discriminating variables are the ‘off-shell’ dilepton mass Mℓ​ℓsubscript𝑀ℓℓM_{\ell\ell}, the polar angle θℓ​ℓsubscript𝜃ℓℓ\theta_{\ell\ell} of the lepton momentum in the rest frame of the off-shell dilepton pair, and the azimuthal angle ΦΦ\Phi between the decay planes of the lepton pairs. These are shown in Fig. 2 and Fig. 3 again assuming a resonance mass mφ=18.4subscript𝑚𝜑18.4m_{\varphi}=18.4 GeV. For each projection we have integrated the other kinematic variables over the phase space Chen and Vega-Morales (2014) defined above and in Fig. 1. We show distributions for the |𝒜1Υ​Υ|2superscriptsuperscriptsubscript𝒜1ΥΥ2|\mathcal{A}_{1}^{\Upsilon\Upsilon}|^{2} (black), |𝒜2Υ​Υ|2superscriptsuperscriptsubscript𝒜2ΥΥ2|\mathcal{A}_{2}^{\Upsilon\Upsilon}|^{2} (blue), |𝒜3Υ​Υ|2superscriptsuperscriptsubscript𝒜3ΥΥ2|\mathcal{A}_{3}^{\Upsilon\Upsilon}|^{2} (turqoise), |𝒜1Υ​Z|2superscriptsuperscriptsubscript𝒜1Υ𝑍2|\mathcal{A}_{1}^{\Upsilon Z}|^{2} (red), |𝒜2Υ​Z|2superscriptsuperscriptsubscript𝒜2Υ𝑍2|\mathcal{A}_{2}^{\Upsilon Z}|^{2} (gold), and |𝒜2Υ​γ|2superscriptsuperscriptsubscript𝒜2Υ𝛾2|\mathcal{A}_{2}^{\Upsilon\gamma}|^{2} (green) ‘diagonal’ terms in Eq. (6). There is also a polar angle for the lepton coming from the on-shell ΥΥ\Upsilon decay which has a very similar distribution to θℓ​ℓsubscript𝜃ℓℓ\theta_{\ell\ell} and also aids in discrimination. From these distributions it is clear that the differential spectra contain information about the pole structure of the operators as well as the C​P𝐶𝑃CP and tensor properties.

Of course the fully differential decay width used in our MEM analysis contains more information than these projections and includes all correlations. Furthermore, utilizing all decay variables aids in discrimination against backgrounds regardless of if they help in signal discrimination. Strong momentum effects in the 𝒜nisuperscriptsubscript𝒜𝑛𝑖\mathcal{A}_{n}^{i} form factors may distort these spectra, but unless there are additional poles they should not drastically change the overall shape of these distributions, particularly the angular ones.

II.4 Opportunities for C​P𝐶𝑃CP violation

Here we briefly discuss some of the potential sources of C​P𝐶𝑃CP violation which might be present in these decays. The possibility of additional poles entering in the form factors 𝒜nisuperscriptsubscript𝒜𝑛𝑖\mathcal{A}_{n}^{i} and generating an imaginary component which can act as a ‘strong phase’ allows for additional sources of C​P𝐶𝑃CP violation apart from those due to interference between C​P𝐶𝑃CP even and C​P𝐶𝑃CP odd operators in Eq. (II.1) with real coefficients. With strong phases present in the 𝒜nisuperscriptsubscript𝒜𝑛𝑖\mathcal{A}_{n}^{i} form factors we can construct C​P𝐶𝑃CP violating observables without requiring four reconstructed four vectors and which can show up for example as an asymmetry in the θℓ​ℓsubscript𝜃ℓℓ\theta_{\ell\ell} distribution Chen et al. (2014c); Li et al. (2015); Chen et al. (2017b). These strong phase C​P𝐶𝑃CP violating effects are in contrast to more standard ‘weak phase’ C​P𝐶𝑃CP violation which occurs when there are C​P𝐶𝑃CP odd and C​P𝐶𝑃CP even operators (such as 𝒜2isuperscriptsubscript𝒜2𝑖\mathcal{A}_{2}^{i} and 𝒜3isuperscriptsubscript𝒜3𝑖\mathcal{A}_{3}^{i}) with the couplings purely real. This type of C​P𝐶𝑃CP violation does require constructing C​P𝐶𝑃CP violating observables via triple products from four reconstructed four vectors and, as is well known from studies of Higgs boson decays to four leptons Cao et al. (2010), manifests as a phase shift in the ΦΦ\Phi distribution.

Of course the presence of poles may also introduce strong momentum effects. A more detailed exploration of all of these possible effects is beyond the scope of this study, should a beautiful tetraquark or some other resonance near the di−ΥΥ-\Upsilon threshold be discovered, it would be interesting to investigate these potential C​P𝐶𝑃CP violating effects and to what extent they can be distinguished.

III MEM Analysis of φ→4​ℓ→𝜑4ℓ\varphi\to 4\ell decays

Here we present results of our MEM analysis to assess the ability of the four lepton channel to probe the nature of a resonance around the di−ΥΥ-\Upsilon threshold. In particular we examine the sensitivity for distinguishing the different operators in Eq. (2) and Eq. (II.1) in order to establish its CP and tensor properties. Details of our analysis procedure based on utilizing the normalized fully differential φ→4​ℓ→𝜑4ℓ\varphi\to 4\ell decay width to construct the likelihood can be found in Stolarski and Vega-Morales (2012); Chen and Vega-Morales (2014); Falkowski and Vega-Morales (2014); Chen et al. (2015d, 2016). To generate events we have implemented the tensor structures in Eq. (2) and Eq. (II.1) into the Feynrules/Madgraph framework Alwall et al. (2014); Christensen and Duhr (2009).

Motivated by the interesting possibility of a bottom tetraquark bound state Berezhnoy et al. (2012); Du et al. (2013); Chen et al. (2015a); Karliner et al. (2017a); Chen et al. (2017a); Bai et al. (2016); Wu et al. (2016); Richard et al. (2017); Wang (2017), for most of our MEM analysis we take the resonance mass to be mφ=18.4subscript𝑚𝜑18.4m_{\varphi}=18.4 GeV corresponding approximately to the lightest predicted mass in Chen et al. (2017a) and assume experimental cuts on the phase space of the final state leptons of Mℓ​ℓ>0.1subscript𝑀ℓℓ0.1M_{\ell\ell}>0.1 GeV, pT​ℓ>2subscript𝑝𝑇ℓ2p_{T\ell}>2 GeV, and ηℓ<2.4subscript𝜂ℓ2.4\eta_{\ell}<2.4 accounting for the relevant selection efficiencies. To attempt to model the background we include the leading order q​q¯→4​ℓ→𝑞¯𝑞4ℓq\bar{q}\to 4\ell process also computed analytically and validated in Chen et al. (2013); Chen and Vega-Morales (2014). There are NLO corrections Frederix et al. (2012); Biedermann et al. (2017) and unknown, but potentially large experimental backgrounds. However, judicious choice of experimental cuts Khachatryan et al. (2016), including requiring an on-shell ΥΥ\Upsilon and a narrow window around the φ𝜑\varphi mass, should greatly reduce the four lepton background. We therefore simply float the background fraction Chen and Vega-Morales (2014); Chen et al. (2015b) and assume during event generation that it is ∼50%similar-toabsentpercent50\sim 50\% at the resonance mass.

We then perform a simplified study to assess how the ability to distinguish between the operators in Eq. (2) and Eq. (II.1) depends on how close the resonance mass is to the di−ΥΥ-\Upsilon threshold and whether it is above or below.

III.1 Hypothesis testing

We use the hypothesis testing techniques developed in De Rujula et al. (2010) and utilized in Stolarski and Vega-Morales (2012); Falkowski and Vega-Morales (2014); Chen et al. (2016) to construct a test statistic that measures the separation power between pairs of operators in Eq. (2) and Eq. (II.1). We do this by constructing the ratio between two likelihoods assuming only one of the operators dominates at a time in each likelihood. Pseudoexperiments are then conducted to obtain a distribution of these likelihood ratios. This is first done assuming one operator as the ‘true’ hypothesis and then repeated assuming the second operator corresponds to the true hypothesis. In each case, a distribution of likelihood ratios is obtained after conducting a large set of psuedoexperiments. The overlap (or lack thereof) between these two distributions can then be converted into a measure of the ability to discriminate between the two models. We follow closely the procedure in Stolarski and Vega-Morales (2012), but present our results in terms of p𝑝p\,-values instead of σ𝜎\sigma.

III.3 Sensitivity around the di−ΥΥ-\Upsilon threshold

We also examine how the ability to distinguish between the operators in Eq. (2) and Eq. (II.1) depends on how close to the di−ΥΥ-\Upsilon threshold the resonance mass is. For this we again perform hypothesis tests for a range of resonance masses around the di−ΥΥ-\Upsilon threshold. In Fig. 5 we show the number of four lepton signal events needed to distinguish the 𝒜1Υ​Υsuperscriptsubscript𝒜1ΥΥ\mathcal{A}_{1}^{\Upsilon\Upsilon} operator from 𝒜2Υ​Υsuperscriptsubscript𝒜2ΥΥ\mathcal{A}_{2}^{\Upsilon\Upsilon} (black), 𝒜3Υ​Υsuperscriptsubscript𝒜3ΥΥ\mathcal{A}_{3}^{\Upsilon\Upsilon} (blue), 𝒜1Υ​Zsuperscriptsubscript𝒜1Υ𝑍\mathcal{A}_{1}^{\Upsilon Z} (red), 𝒜2Υ​Zsuperscriptsubscript𝒜2Υ𝑍\mathcal{A}_{2}^{\Upsilon Z} (turqoise), 𝒜2Υ​γsuperscriptsubscript𝒜2Υ𝛾\mathcal{A}_{2}^{\Upsilon\gamma} (forest green), 𝒜3Υ​Zsuperscriptsubscript𝒜3Υ𝑍\mathcal{A}_{3}^{\Upsilon Z} (dark green), 𝒜3Υ​γsuperscriptsubscript𝒜3Υ𝛾\mathcal{A}_{3}^{\Upsilon\gamma} (pink) or vice versa with 95%percent9595\% confidence versus the four lepton resonance mass. The points correspond to the values for the resonance mass of mφ=18.4,18.8,18.9,18.92​(threshold),18.94,19.0,19.3subscript𝑚𝜑18.418.818.918.92threshold18.9419.019.3m_{\varphi}=18.4,18.8,18.9,18.92\,(\rm{threshold}),18.94,19.0,19.3 GeV. In this case we have neglected backgrounds and assumed 5 signal events for the 2​e​2​μ2𝑒2𝜇2e2\mu channel and 5 events for 4​e+4​μ4𝑒4𝜇4e+4\mu channel before combining into one likelihood Chen et al. (2015d, 2016) formed out of the φ→4​ℓ→𝜑4ℓ\varphi\to 4\ell fully differential decay width.

We see the ability to distinguish between the different operators in Eq. (2) and Eq. (II.1) depends quite strongly on the exact resonance mass. In general we see that the operator most difficult to distinguish from 𝒜1Υ​Υsuperscriptsubscript𝒜1ΥΥ\mathcal{A}_{1}^{\Upsilon\Upsilon}, is 𝒜2Υ​Υsuperscriptsubscript𝒜2ΥΥ\mathcal{A}_{2}^{\Upsilon\Upsilon} which has the same C​P𝐶𝑃CP and pole structure. Furthermore, we see that the nearer to threshold the more difficult it is to distinguish these two operators. Interestingly, we see that distinguishing between 𝒜1Υ​Υsuperscriptsubscript𝒜1ΥΥ\mathcal{A}_{1}^{\Upsilon\Upsilon} and the C​P𝐶𝑃CP odd 𝒜3Υ​Υsuperscriptsubscript𝒜3ΥΥ\mathcal{A}_{3}^{\Upsilon\Upsilon} operator is much easier and becomes more so the closer to the di-ΥΥ\Upsilon threshold we get. This is due to a combination of the ΦΦ\Phi distribution (see Fig. 3) as well as the di-lepton invariant mass distribution (Mℓ​ℓsubscript𝑀ℓℓM_{\ell\ell}), which in general is not peaked at the same mass and has a different slope than the Mℓ​ℓsubscript𝑀ℓℓM_{\ell\ell} distribution in 𝒜1Υ​Υsuperscriptsubscript𝒜1ΥΥ\mathcal{A}_{1}^{\Upsilon\Upsilon} and 𝒜2Υ​Υsuperscriptsubscript𝒜2ΥΥ\mathcal{A}_{2}^{\Upsilon\Upsilon}. In other words, the Mℓ​ℓsubscript𝑀ℓℓM_{\ell\ell} distribution also contains information about the C​P𝐶𝑃CP properties of the φ​Υ​Υ𝜑ΥΥ\varphi\Upsilon\Upsilon distribution (see Fig. 2) which of course has also been noted in SM-like Higgs boson decays to four leptons Boughezal et al. (2012). In general we see that, depending on the nature and mass of a putative resonance around the di-ΥΥ\Upsilon threshold, just a few handful of signal events may be sufficient for deciphering its C​P𝐶𝑃CP and tensor properties in the four lepton channel.

The strong dependence on the exact resonance implies that detector resolution effects may play an important role and need to be included for a precise quantification of the sensitivity. While we have not done so for this preliminary study, including these detector effects can be done with the framework developed in Chen et al. (2015b, 2014b) which we leave to future work should a four lepton resonance be discovered near the di-ΥΥ\Upsilon threshold.

III.4 Opportunities for parameter extraction

One of our primary assumptions in this analysis is that only one operator from Eq. (2) and Eq. (II.1) dominates at a time. We have also neglected any momentum dependence in the form factors as well as parton showering and jets. These assumptions of course may not be true in reality and therefore an analysis which can extract multiple form factors simultaneously with possible momentum dependence is also important. Furthermore, as Fig. 1 shows, even if one operator dominates, interference effects may be relevant which would allow us to probe a multitude of effects. As more four lepton events are collected, utilizing multi-parameter fitting methods Chen and Vega-Morales (2014); Chen et al. (2015b) to extract the various form factors may become possible and also opens the possibility of probing C​P𝐶𝑃CP violating effects. However, this requires a more careful examination of backgrounds and detector resolution effects so we leave this to interesting possibilities which can be explored should a four lepton resonance around ∼18−19similar-toabsent1819\sim 18-19 GeV be observed.

IV Summary and conclusions

Motivated by recent studies Berezhnoy et al. (2012); Du et al. (2013); Chen et al. (2015a); Karliner et al. (2017a); Chen et al. (2017a); Bai et al. (2016); Wu et al. (2016); Richard et al. (2017); Wang (2017); Eichten and Liu (2017) which indicate that a QCD state composed of four bottom quarks may be present around around the di−ΥΥ-\Upsilon threshold in the range ∼18−19similar-toabsent1819\sim 18-19 GeV and potentially observable in its decays to four leptons, we have explored the possibility of using the normalized fully differential decay width to four leptons to probe its underlying nature and in particular its C​P𝐶𝑃CP and tensor properties. Further motivated by the exciting possibility of a tetraquark bound state explored in some of these studies Berezhnoy et al. (2012); Du et al. (2013); Chen et al. (2015a); Karliner et al. (2017a); Chen et al. (2017a); Bai et al. (2016); Wu et al. (2016); Richard et al. (2017); Wang (2017), we emphasize in our analysis a spin-0 resonance ∼500similar-toabsent500\sim 500 MeV below the di-ΥΥ\Upsilon threshold, but also examine resonance masses just below, at, and above the di-ΥΥ\Upsilon threshold.

Assuming the putative resonance is a spin-0 boson, we have performed a simplified matrix element method analysis based on hypothesis testing and assuming its decays are dominated by one effective operator at a time. We find excellent prospects at the LHC once 𝒪​(10−100)𝒪10100\mathcal{O}(10-100) signal events are collected indicating that perhaps its underlying nature, including C​P𝐶𝑃CP properties, can be directly probed. This may in principle be possible not long after discovery depending on (unknown) production cross sections and the exact resonance mass. We have also briefly discussed various potential C​P𝐶𝑃CP violating effects which might be present in these decays, but we leave a study of this as well as spin-1 and spin-2 resonances to ongoing Chen et al. (2017c) and future work.

While this study is motivated by possible four bottom quark QCD states near the di−ΥΥ-\Upsilon threshold, our analysis methods easily generalize to any potential light scalar, including other multi-quark states, which can decay to four lepton final states at the LHC or other colliders.
