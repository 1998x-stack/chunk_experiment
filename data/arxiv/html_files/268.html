Abstract

Dark Matter (DM) and Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND) models of rotationally supported galaxies lead to curves with different geometries in (gN,gt​o​t)subscript𝑔𝑁subscript𝑔𝑡𝑜𝑡(g_{N},g_{tot})-space (g​2𝑔2g2-space). Here gt​o​tsubscript𝑔𝑡𝑜𝑡g_{tot} is the total acceleration and gNsubscript𝑔𝑁g_{N} is the acceleration as obtained from the baryonic matter via Newtonian dynamics. In MOND modified inertia (MI) models the curves in g​2𝑔2g2-space are closed with zero area and so curve segments at radii r≥rN𝑟subscript𝑟𝑁r\geq r_{N} (large radii) and r<rN𝑟subscript𝑟𝑁r<r_{N} (small radii) coincide, where rNsubscript𝑟𝑁r_{N} is the radius where gNsubscript𝑔𝑁g_{N} is greatest. In DM models with cored density profiles where gt​o​tsubscript𝑔𝑡𝑜𝑡g_{tot} is also zero at the galactic centre, the curves are again closed, but the area of the closed curves are in general non-zero because the curve segments at radii r≥rN𝑟subscript𝑟𝑁r\geq r_{N} and r<rN𝑟subscript𝑟𝑁r<r_{N} do not coincide. Finally in DM models with cuspy density profiles such as the NFW profile where gt​o​tsubscript𝑔𝑡𝑜𝑡g_{tot} is formally non-zero at the galactic origin the curves are open, and again the curve segments at radii r≥rN𝑟subscript𝑟𝑁r\geq r_{N} and r<rN𝑟subscript𝑟𝑁r<r_{N} do not coincide.
We develop a test of whether data at small and large radii coincide and investigate rotation curves from the SPARC database in order to discriminate between the above geometries. Due to loosely quantified systematic uncertainties we do not underline the result of the test, but instead conclude that the test illustrates the relevance of this type of analysis and demonstrate the ability to discriminate between the considered DM and MI models in this way.

1 Introduction

There is a significant amount of astrophysical evidence for missing gravity on scales ranging from galactic to cosmological. The evidence includes the measured rotation curves of galaxies (Rubin & Ford, 1970; Bosma, 1978; Rubin et al., 1980) which appear to be well described by a modification of Newtonian dynamics (MOND) for accelerations below a characteristic acceleration scale close to the value a0∼c​H0similar-tosubscript𝑎0𝑐subscript𝐻0a_{0}\sim cH_{0}, where c𝑐c is the speed of light and H0subscript𝐻0H_{0} is the value of the Hubble constant today (Milgrom, 1983). In particular MOND provides an explanation of the Tully-Fisher relation (Tully & Fisher, 1977) and successfully predicted the Baryonic Tully-Fisher relation  (McGaugh et al., 2000). On larger scales the lensing of galaxy clusters (Mellier et al., 1989), observations of cluster mergers (Clowe et al., 2006) and large scale structure surveys (Dodelson & Liguori, 2006) all appear consistent with the DM hypothesis while it is not obvious how to explain this body of observations within the MOND hypothesis.

MOND models can arise from a modification of gravity (MOND modified gravity, MG) or a modification of inertia (MOND modified inertia, MI) (Milgrom, 1983). In a recent analysis (McGaugh et al., 2016) it was found that the rotation curve data from the galaxies in the SPARC database (Lelli et al., 2016) follow an analytical relation (called the radial acceleration relation (RAR)) closely related to MI. Data from all galaxies in the SPARC database are pooled together in the analysis of McGaugh et al. (2016). The pooling af data wash out radial information from each galaxy. In their analysis Petersen & Lelli (2020) have shown that the radial information hold the potential to discriminate between MI and MG. In this article we will analyze the radial information in an attempt to discriminate between MI and DM. It will be shown how dark matter (DM) and MI lead to curves with different geometries in (gN,gt​o​t)subscript𝑔𝑁subscript𝑔𝑡𝑜𝑡(g_{N},g_{tot})-space (g​2𝑔2g2-space) (section 111). Here gt​o​tsubscript𝑔𝑡𝑜𝑡g_{tot} is the total centripetal acceleration and gNsubscript𝑔𝑁g_{N} is the centripetal acceleration as obtained from the baryonic matter via Newtonian dynamics – both for rotationally supported galaxies. The differences in geometry are also apparent from e.g. Salucci (2016), but in the present study we explore how the differences may be used to test MI (sections 2−4242-4).

Previous analyses of rotation curve data from the SPARC database (Lelli et al., 2016) in g​2𝑔2g2-space has found it to be both consistent with MI (McGaugh et al., 2016; Lelli et al., 2017) and with DM  (Di Cintio & Lelli, 2016; Salucci, 2016; Keller & Wadsley, 2017; Ludlow et al., 2017). See also Desmond (2017) for a detailed statistical analysis of the SPARC data. This is a priori not at odds with MI and DM yielding different geometries in g​2𝑔2g2-space since the differences manifests themselves in each galaxy mainly at small radii r≲rNless-than-or-similar-to𝑟subscript𝑟𝑁r\lesssim r_{N}, where rNsubscript𝑟𝑁r_{N} is the radius of the maximum Newtonian baryonic acceleration gNsubscript𝑔𝑁g_{N}. The SPARC database is by far dominated by points at large radii (r≳rb​a​rgreater-than-or-equivalent-to𝑟subscript𝑟𝑏𝑎𝑟r\gtrsim r_{bar} and so analyses that do not retain radial information (global analyses) will mostly test the behavior at large radii - at which the theoretical predictions of dark matter and MI are similar, namely gt​o​t∼gNsimilar-tosubscript𝑔𝑡𝑜𝑡subscript𝑔𝑁g_{tot}\sim\sqrt{g_{N}} – dictated by the baryonic Tully-Fisher relation (Tully & Fisher, 1977). Hence, with data from the SPARC database, the global analyses are not optimal for distinguishing between DM and MI. A better approach is to investigate the behavior of galaxies at small radii relative to large radii – that is the radial information from each galaxy is needed in order to distinguish between different models.
In this manuscript, we use t​o​t𝑡𝑜𝑡tot and N𝑁N subscripts to denote theoretical predictions for quantities with subscript o​b​s𝑜𝑏𝑠obs and b​a​r𝑏𝑎𝑟bar, respectively.

This is the core idea of this article and what will be tested in the following using data from the SPARC database.

6 Summary and Discussion

In this article the g​2𝑔2g2-space geometries of MOND modified inertia (MI) including the RAR (equation (6)) and NFW and isothermal (ISO) dark matter (DM) density profiles have been reviewed and discussed. It has been shown how MI leads to a g​2𝑔2g2-space geometry that is covered twice with radius (the MI g​2𝑔2g2-space values for large and small radii coincide) whereas both ISO and NFW DM lead to g​2𝑔2g2-space geometries that are covered only once (the ISO and NFW DM g​2𝑔2g2-space values for large and small radii do not coincide). Using this knowledge a test of whether data at small and large radii coincide is developed and applied in order to discriminate between MI on one side and NFW and ISO dark matter on the other.

Using data from the SPARC database, and under the approximations and assumptions made (e.g. regarding mass to light ratios being constant and having no radial dependence as well as estimating the expected value for MI in the described way), the test disfavors MI with ≳8​σgreater-than-or-equivalent-toabsent8𝜎\gtrsim 8\sigma, where "≳greater-than-or-equivalent-to\gtrsim" account for the approximation in using the RAR to estimate the expected value of MI in general. The expectation value of MI used here increases the χ2superscript𝜒2\chi^{2} by 9%percent99\% from χ2≈375superscript𝜒2375\chi^{2}\approx 375 to χ2≈408superscript𝜒2408\chi^{2}\approx 408 as specified in section 5. To facilitate discussion we define χr​e​f2≡408subscriptsuperscript𝜒2𝑟𝑒𝑓408\chi^{2}_{ref}\equiv 408 as a reference value. In order for MI to be disfavored by less than 3​σ3𝜎3\sigma, χr​e​f2subscriptsuperscript𝜒2𝑟𝑒𝑓\chi^{2}_{ref} must be lowered by ≳69%greater-than-or-similar-toabsentpercent69\ga 69\% with fixed degrees of freedom. Hence, it would likely require a combination of 1) a different definition of rb​a​rsubscript𝑟𝑏𝑎𝑟r_{bar}, 2) different values for the mass to light ratios, possibly including radial dependence, 3) removal of statistical outliers and 4) other effects.
Regarding point 1: There is an unaccounted bias in that the bins are defined by rb​a​rsubscript𝑟𝑏𝑎𝑟r_{bar}, a point which is affected by uncertainty. The impact of this bias can be roughly estimated by moving rb​a​rsubscript𝑟𝑏𝑎𝑟r_{bar} one point toward smaller (larger) radii. Doing so yields a 52%​(−13%)percent52percent1352\%\,(-13\%) reduction in χ2superscript𝜒2\chi^{2} relative to χr​e​f2subscriptsuperscript𝜒2𝑟𝑒𝑓\chi^{2}_{ref}, however with the accompanying degrees of freedom 44​(79)447944\,(79). The reduction in degrees of freedom (galaxies) are due to the criterion of at least two points at small radii. Considering the reduced chi square changes from χr​e​f285=4.8subscriptsuperscript𝜒2𝑟𝑒𝑓854.8\frac{\chi^{2}_{ref}}{85}=4.8 to χr​e​f2​(1−0.52)44=4.4subscriptsuperscript𝜒2𝑟𝑒𝑓10.52444.4\frac{\chi^{2}_{ref}(1-0.52)}{44}=4.4 after the shift to smaller radii, it can be concluded that although the shift in rb​a​rsubscript𝑟𝑏𝑎𝑟r_{bar} greatly affect the χ2superscript𝜒2\chi^{2} it only slightly reduce the statistical disfavoring of MI.
Regarding point 2: The dependence on the mass to light ratios may significantly affect gb​a​rsubscript𝑔𝑏𝑎𝑟g_{bar} and consequently both the expected value of MI and the specification of rb​a​rsubscript𝑟𝑏𝑎𝑟r_{bar}. The fixed mass to light ratios used here (Υ~id=0.5subscriptsuperscript~Υ𝑑𝑖0.5\tilde{\Upsilon}^{d}_{i}=0.5 and Υ~ib=0.7subscriptsuperscript~Υ𝑏𝑖0.7\tilde{\Upsilon}^{b}_{i}=0.7) are not optimal for all galaxies. This is clear from the rotation curve plots in appendix C, where for some galaxies vb​a​r>vo​b​ssubscript𝑣𝑏𝑎𝑟subscript𝑣𝑜𝑏𝑠v_{bar}>v_{obs} for limited radial range (e.g. as is the case for CAMB and NGC4217). To roughly gauge the effect of radically different mass to light ratios, we take Υ~id=Υ~ib=0.2subscriptsuperscript~Υ𝑑𝑖subscriptsuperscript~Υ𝑏𝑖0.2\tilde{\Upsilon}^{d}_{i}=\tilde{\Upsilon}^{b}_{i}=0.2 (corresponding to galaxies dominated by DM at all radii (Lelli et al., 2017)), yielding an 8%percent88\% increase in the χ2superscript𝜒2\chi^{2} relative to χr​e​f2subscriptsuperscript𝜒2𝑟𝑒𝑓\chi^{2}_{ref} (the result become slightly more significant). This is however just an example and one can imagine changing individual mass to light ratios and uncertainties potentially having a great impact on the result.
Regarding point 3: Figure 5 shows the distribution of residuals. Of the galaxies with |Ri|>4subscript𝑅𝑖4|R_{i}|>4 UGC11455, NGC4217, NGC3109, ESO563-G021 and D631-7 have quality flag 1 whereas CAMB have quality flag 2 (listed in the SPARC database). This indicates that the disfavoring of MI is not rooted in bad data (figure 6 in appendix B show residuals after discarding all galaxies with quality flag larger than 1). That being said, as mentioned in point 2, the fixed mass to light ratios used are too large in case of NGC4217 and CAMB and possibly also affect the innermost radii of UGC11455 and ESO563-G021. It can also be argued that the high residual value in case of ESO563-G021 is at least partially rooted in a peculiar selection of points (e.g. see figure 7 in appendix C). Hence, although data are of good quality, for several of the significant outliers, there are valid arguments that could at least partially explain the high residuals. To be conservative and test the dependence on the outliers, we proceed to discard all galaxies with |Ri|>4subscript𝑅𝑖4|R_{i}|>4. Doing so yield a 42%percent4242\% drop in the χ2superscript𝜒2\chi^{2} relative to χr​e​f2subscriptsuperscript𝜒2𝑟𝑒𝑓\chi^{2}_{ref}, with an associated drop in degrees of freedom from 858585 to 797979.
Regarding point 4: Another factor that potentially could affect the conclusion is that we take the uncertainties from the SPARC database at face value and propagate these to the observables constructed in this article. Significant uncertainty in the uncertainties may therefore have a potentially significant impact on the conclusion. Quantifying this systematic uncertainty is beyond the scope of this paper. Instead we emphasize that all quantitative results in this paper rely on the reliability of the reported uncertainties of data from the SPARC database.
Combining points 1-3 by first changing the mass to light ratio, then shifting rb​a​rsubscript𝑟𝑏𝑎𝑟r_{bar} one point toward smaller radii and lastly discarding galaxies with |Ri|>4subscript𝑅𝑖4|R_{i}|>4, yield a 66%percent6666\% reduction in χ2superscript𝜒2\chi^{2} relative to χr​e​f2subscriptsuperscript𝜒2𝑟𝑒𝑓\chi^{2}_{ref}, however, with an associated reduction in degrees of freedom from 858585 to 474747. Hence, even in this case MI is still disfavored by ≈6.5​σabsent6.5𝜎\approx 6.5\sigma. That being said, points 1-3 are roughly estimated (and point 4 not quantified) and so instead of underlining the numerical result, we conclude that our test illustrates the relevance of analyses that include radial information and demonstrates the ability to discriminate between the considered DM and MI models. Lastly, we emphasize that MI models do not constitute all MOND models. MOND modified gravity (MG) models (another class of MOND models) lead to g​2𝑔2g2-space geometries similar to those of ISO DM and may still be consistent with our results. From figure 5 it is clear that there is an overweight of outliers with Ri>3subscript𝑅𝑖3R_{i}>3 relative to Ri<−3subscript𝑅𝑖3R_{i}<-3. This indicate that go​b​ssubscript𝑔𝑜𝑏𝑠g_{obs} for small radii may be systematically lower than go​b​ssubscript𝑔𝑜𝑏𝑠g_{obs} at large radii. This tendency is in line with what is expected from both ISO DM and MG (Banik et al., 2018; Eriksen et al., 2019).
